Narrative

Nancy Ellen Mechem seems to have led the idealized life of a devout Protestant farm woman, raising her children and supporting her husband in a quiet rural section of Ohio, one of perhaps the last generation of Americans before life became urban and industrialized. Although she lived through the Civil War, her life seems to have been little affected by it. Rather, for her, the strongest national force to change her life seems to have been upwelling of Protestant devotion known as the "Second Great Awakening," which swept the country starting in the 1840s when she was a girl. Her life would be one of motherhood toward her seven children, aiding her husband on their farm, and helping both her father and then continuing his work in the creation of the new Protestant "Christian Church" in Belmont Ridge. This faith would motivate three of her sons to become ministers, and would strongly influence her grandchildren and their descendants. Like her husband, she was highly respected and well loved throughout her community.

Nancy was born to Lewis and Bathsheba Mechem on 14 January 1838, the fifth of ten children. Her father was a farmer in Belmont County throughout his life. Both of her parents were strongly involved with founding the Belmont Ridge Christian Church. The organization to which this church belonged, the Disciples of Christ, originated in the Restorationism movement of the early 1800s that strove to downplay the split of the Christianity into separate denominations. It was a direct product of the Second Great Awakening, which spread Protestant fervor throughout the country, and spread a welcoming gospel of forgiveness and redemption, rather than the stern hellfire Christianity favored by eighteenth-century Protestants.

As Nancy grew up, her parents held meetings of the Christian Church in their home, because no church building had been constructed for the Disciples of Christ in their town. At these meetings, she no doubt came across the young Erastus Moore, who also was a follower of the Christian Church, and a member of the Christian Church community before the church building was erected. In 1857, the church community contracted Erastus and his brother, "Add" Moore, to build a church. It was finished in 1858, the same year that Nancy and Erastus were married. Perhaps they were married in the very church that Erastus had built.

Nancy's life was dedicated to raising her family and building the church community - two pursuits which seem to have been successful. All but one of her seven children lived to adulthood, and three of them would become ministers with the Disciples of Christ.

Nancy died in 1909, after 51 years of marriage with Erastus. She was known for her gentle, kind, and pleasant Christian disposition. Her obituary commented upon the gentle, kindly, Christian influence that emanated from her in the home, in the community, and in the church. A large number of her friends and relatives were present when, after the services in the church, she was laid her to rest in the cemetery at Belmont Ridge, Ohio.
