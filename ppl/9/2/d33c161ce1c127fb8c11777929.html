Narrative

Katherine Martin "Kate" Leach was born 27 Mar 1867 in Canton, Illinois, where her father, William "W.E." Leach, a Civil War veteran who had grown up in Michigan and moved to Illinois as a young man, worked as a clerk for the railroad. Her mother was Catherine Hipple Martin, who was born in Indiana, the daughter of a physician. Kate was the second of eight surviving children.

Kate's childhood was that of a devout Baptist family. Her parents were both active members of their church were sufficiently respected in the community to have small newspaper articles published of their comings and goings when they moved between towns. The obituaries of her parents mentioned how strongly they had participated in their church communities. Later, when she was grown, she refused to drink alcohol and raised her children as devout Baptists.

Her family moved from Canton to Galesburg by 1884. There, she noticed Henry M. Tompkins, a young Galesburg man who had left high school to help support his family. She was a year younger than he was, and the two fell in love, most likely without the approval of Henry's father, Franklin. Kate became pregnant while 17, and she and Henry hoped to marry, but because they were under age, they needed Franklin's consent. Franklin steadfastly refused. By April 1885, Kate was nine months pregnant, and still he refused. Then, on 22 April, 26 days after Kate turned 18, three momentous events took place on the same day: her fiancee's father, Franklin, died (which event freed Kate and Henry to marry), Kate and Henry immediately married, and she gave birth to their first child. Henry's mother signed the marriage certificate for Henry, while W.E. signed the certificate for Kate.

Kate and Henry had four children, Albert, Kate, Ruth, and Paul, all born in Galesburg. Sadly, Albert, the boy born on the day of their marriage, died as a toddler. He was walking down stairs when a girl going more quickly behind him bumped him, and he fell.
Henry, despite having only attended school until the age of about 15, rose professionally within the Illinois and Michigan railroads, including lines operated by the New York Central Railroad. As a result, they moved between the cities serviced by the New York Central's "water route" which provided fast passenger and freight service from New York City to the plains via the southern edge of the great lakes. After Henry was hired by the Lakeshore and Michigan Southern Railroad, they moved to Cleveland for a year, in about 1909. Next, they moved to Buffalo, New York, where they lived for several years as Henry worked as an inspector for a subsidiary of the New York Central Railroad. Finally, they returned to Cleveland, where they lived for the rest of their lives. From Cleveland, Henry continued to commute often by train to New York City.
In each of the cities, Kate worked as a homemaker to raise their three children. She and Henry had a close relationship and were devoted to one another. She, like Henry, was a strong member of their Baptist church, and widely respected within their community.
Often, when Henry had to travel to New York, Kate would travel there too to see the city with him. Sadly, in 1927 on one such visit, Kate suffered a severe stroke. She had gone to their hotel room while Henry was at work. When he returned, he was unable to open the door, and he and others had to break it down. Inside, they found Kate collapsed on the floor. Because of Henry's position at the railroad, he was given a private rail car to transport her back to Cleveland. The railroad workers had to remove a window from the car for Kate to be inserted into the car on a stretcher. A doctor and nurse traveled with them in the car from New York to Cleveland. When they arrived in Cleveland, Henry's son-in-law, Ralph Trinkner, who also worked for the railroad, had arranged for railroad men to remove the window and help remove Kate from the car. She survived for 12 more years, but never regained her full capacities. Slowly, she learned to speak a few phrases and could sit up in bed. After time, she was able to walk around the house, so long as she had a railing or furniture to steady herself. Henry was devoted to her, and installed railings throughout the house for her.
In 1928, Kate's son-in-law, Frederick Teal, passed away. He was the husband of her daughter, Kate Olive Tompkins. She and her two daughters, Dorothy and Helen, moved to live with Kate and Henry for several years, and helped Kate recover as best she could from her stroke. Kate passed away in 1939 in Cleveland.
