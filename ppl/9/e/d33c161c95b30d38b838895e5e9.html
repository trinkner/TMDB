Narrative

Frederick William Berger experienced much of the stereotypical American immigrant life of the late 1800s. He was born and raised in Germany, took up arms in the Civil War very quickly after immigrating into the U.S., experienced the urbanization and industrialization of Cleveland, married a fellow immigrant, knew the tragedy of a parent who lossed his first two children in their infancy, and successfully carved out a living of hard blue-collar work as a teamster that provided for his wife and five surviving children. He died in 1904, just as the motor car was developing into the technology that ultimately would replace his occupation.

Frederick was born in the Kingdom of Prussia, and came to the States nine years before Germany unified under the Prussian leader, Bismarck. Frederick was possibly born in the Polish area of Prussia, as he indicated "Poland - Prussia" as his native country on the birth return document of one of his children. On all other documents found so far, Frederick indicated either "Germany" or "Prussia." He came to the United States in 1862 or 1863 at the age of 21, during the first years of the Civil War. He likely sailed on the ship the Clio, sailing from Hamburg and arriving in New York on 4 Aug 1863. On the ship manifest, he is listed as a "farmer." Often ship manifests list every German man as a "farmer," but in this case the manifest lists different occupations for most of the men, so "farmer" probably was his profession at this early stage in his life.

Frederick apparently had difficulty in adjusting his German given name to the American custom of a single first name, as he went by several first names: "Frederick," "Fred," "William," "William Frederick," and "Frederick William." This confusion is explained by the German tradition of giving children two first names: one ceremonial and one common name for daily use. Fred was probably born "Wilhelm Friedrich Berger" or "Friedrich Wilhelm Berger."

Fred evidently settled quickly in Ohio. He would spend the rest of his life living in Cleveland, and joined an Ohio regiment to fight in th Civil War. He served as a private late in the war, enlisting with the 81st Ohio Infantry on 18 March 1865, just a month before the end of the war, and serving until 13 July 1865, three months after the surrender of the South. During that time, the 81st Infantry fought in the deep South, where it participated in the occupation of Goldsboro, the advance on Raleigh, the occupation of Raleigh, the battle of Bennett's House, and finally the surrender of Johnston and his army. The unit marched to Washington, D.C., via Richmond between 19 April and 20 May, and participated in the Grand Review on 24 May. It moved to Louisville, Kentucky in June, and there mustered out 13 July 13 1865. It is possible that Fred also served early in the war, as a record exists for a "Fred Berger" who enlisted in the Ohio 106th Infantry in 1862; I have not been able to find evidence to corroborate that this record pertains to our family's Frederick Berger.

After the war, in the late 1860s and early 1870s, Fred worked at the Cleveland Paper Company, where he met an older Dutch immigrant, Dirk Jan Bent, who also worked there. Dirk likely introduced his daughter Berendina to Fred, as Fred married her in Cleveland on 3 August 1868. "Dena" was from the eastern Netherlands town of Wisch, very near the border with Germany, and so she might have spoken some German. We don't know which language they spoke in their home. Both immigrants, they apparently met in Cleveland, and lived there throughout their adult lives.

Fred and Dena most likely led a life of humble material means, as Fred worked much as Dena's father had, as a relatively poorly paid laborer. In the 1860s, he worked in the paper factory, and by the early 1870s, he worked as a teamster. Teamsters, at the time, drove wagons or carts pulled by horses or oxen to deliver goods throughout the city. Perhaps he worked delivering paper for the Cleveland Paper Company, mentioned above. According to Edna Trinkner, Fred and Dena had a dairy farm in or near Cleveland, which they ran until a fire was accidentally started in their barn by a tramp in 1885. The city directory of Cleveland shows that Fred listed himself as a "teamster" in various years between 1872 and 1904, although the directory does not mention him durign most of the 1880s, when perhaps he and Dena were operating a farm. Perhaps Fred offered his teamster services as a side job, since a dairy farm would have likely had a cart and a horse. By 1900, Fred and Dena were renting a house, which suggests that either Fred made relatively low wages, or that they had fallen upon harder times perhaps because of the burning of their dairy. By 1904, Fred had retired.

Life for Fred in America was not easy. By 1900, he and Dena had had 12 children, but sadly seven had died. Records provide for the names of at least nine children, including young Dena, who died of "teething" at age nine months, and William, who died after just three days. According to family memory, another child, an infant girl, died as an indirect result of the fire in Fred and Dena's barn in 1885. Family memory also holds that a three-year old boy died when he fell into a pail of scalding water. Dena and Fred's eldest daughter, Katheryn married Paul W. Trinkner. The other children who survived to adulthood were William, Charles, Amelia and Edward (whose legal names seems to have been Adam). Sadly, Edward committed suicide by poison in 1915, three years after Dena died, when his wife was unfaithful.

Fred and Dena lived first on Forest Street and then on Croton Street in Cleveland, always just a few houses away from Dena's father, Dirk Jan Bent, who also lived on Forest and Croton Streets. Fred died in the city in 1904, at the age of 63, of leucocythoemie.
