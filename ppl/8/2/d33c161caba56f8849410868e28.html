Narrative

Many Dutch painters of the 1800s show a beautiful pastoral countryside, windswept seascapes, and picturesque village streets. Other paintings, however, depict grinding poverty and village squalor. Although we don't know much about Berendina Bent's early Dutch childhood in a rural village, it was probably sufficiently squalid to persuade the family to take a dramatic chance on immigration to the U.S.
"Dena," named after her mother, was born to a poor family in eastern Netherlands on 1 July 1851. Her father, Dirk Jan Bent, was a day laborer. We don't know much about the family, but they were likely quite poor and lived in Wisch, a very small village in Gelderland, on the eastern edge of the Kingdom of the Netherlands very close to the border the with Prussia. It's likely that they lived a life similar to that shown in van Gogh's paintings of Dutch villagers, perhaps like the family shown in "The Potato Eaters." The family brought only two children to America: Dena and her sister, Clara, who was two years older. Because Dena's father was listed as a "laborer" rather than as a farmer, they likely lived and worked in the village itself, did not own their own land, and depended upon the local economy to provide opportunities for work. They would have been hit hard by any downturn in the local economy, and probably could not have grown their own food on a farm. Life for the poor in Europe at mid-century was often affected by shifting politics, increasing taxes, the loss of jobs due to industrialization, and outbreaks of disease such as cholera. We don't know the specifics of why the family left for America, but they decided to leave Wisch in 1856, when Dena was just four.
The family left from the port city of Rotterdam on the "Epaminondas," an 1,125-ton wooden ship trimmed in yellow and captained by Captain H. Kerr. It's possible that the adventure of crossing the Atlantic would have been one of Dena's earliest memories. Like most immigrants at the time, her family probably had to sell most of their possessions to afford the passage. They arrived at New York on 26 May 1856, during the times of sectionalist political upheaval that would lead to Lincoln's election and the eruption of the Civil War. Sadly, en route, Dena's young brother, Sevrein, died at the age of nine months.
Within four years of their arrival, Dena and the family were living in Cleveland, Ohio. Perhaps they chose Cleveland because of the large Germanic community already established there. Although the family spoke Dutch, they may also have spoken some German. In the 1860 census, Dena was listed as attending school, so she likely was much more fluent in English than her parents.
On 3 August 1868, at the age of just 17 and three years after the Civil War, she married Frederick William Berger. He was a 27-year-old German from Prussia who had come in the U.S. in 1862 and fought briefly in the war. In the late 1860s he worked at the Cleveland Paper Company, the same factory where Dena's father worked. We don't know which language they spoke at home. Both immigrants, they apparently met in Cleveland, and lived there throughout their adult lives.
Fred and Dena most likely led a life of humble material means, as Fred worked much as Dena's father had, as a relatively poorly paid laborer. In the 1860s, he worked in the paper factory, and by the early 1870s, he worked as a teamster. Teamsters, at the time, drove wagons or carts pulled by horses or oxen to deliver goods throughout the city. Perhaps he worked delivering paper for the Cleveland Paper Company, mentioned above. According to Edna Trinkner, Fred and Dena had a dairy farm in or near Cleveland, which they ran until a fire was accidentally started in their barn by a tramp in 1885. The city directory of Cleveland shows that Fred listed himself as a "teamster" in various years between 1872 and 1904, although the directory does not mention him durign most of the 1880s, when perhaps he and Dena were operating a farm. It's also possible that Fred offered his teamster services as a side job, since a dairy farm would have likely had a cart and a horse. By 1900, Fred and Dena were renting a house, which suggests that either Fred made relatively low wages, or that they had fallen upon harder times perhaps because of the burning of their dairy. By 1904, Fred had retired. Dena apparently worked as a homemaker, staying home throughout her life to care for the children.
Although life for Dena was likely better in Cleveland than it would have been in their small rural village in the Netherlands, her American life was not easy. By 1900 when she was 49, she had given birth to 12 children, but sadly seven had died. Records provide for the names of at least nine children, including young Dena, who died of "teething" at age nine months, and William, who died after just three days. According to family memory, another child, an infant girl, died as an indirect result of the fire in Fred and Dena's barn in 1885. Family memory also holds that a three-year old boy died when he fell into a pail of scalding water.
Dena and Fred's eldest daughter, Kathryn married Paul W. Trinkner. The other children who survived to adulthood were William, Charles, Amelia and Edward (whose legal names seems to have been Adam). Sadly, Edward committed suicide by poison in 1915, three years after Dena died, when his wife was unfaithful.
Fred and Dena lived first on Forest Street and then on Croton Street in Cleveland, always just a few houses away from Dena's father, Dirk Jan Bent, who also lived on Forest and Croton Streets. After Fred died in 1904, Dena continued as the head of the household, living with her sons Charles and William, as well as Charles's wife and daughter. Eight years after Fred died, Dena died of stomach cancer in Cleveland on 5 October 1912 at the age of 61.
