Narrative

Malinda Neville was a child of the Revolution, and her 91-year life encapsulated the westerward growth of the nation. Born into a family that fervently supported the Revolution, she participated in the settling of Kentucky, Tennessee, and Missouri, and finally took the long westward journey by covered wagon to the gold region of California. She died five years after the Civil War, and watched one of her sons serve as a state senator in Califiornia.

Malinda Neville was probably born about 1778 in Rutherford County, North Carolina, the ninth child of William Neville and Winifred Oldham. Her father served with distinction in the Revolutionary War, retiring as a captain. He also served in the French and Indian War.

Malinda married David Thompson in Mercer county, Kentucky on 3 December 1796. Her parents signed their consent for her marriage, which implies that she was under age at the time of her marriage. If we assume that she was 17 at the time of her marriage, she would have been born about 1778. Her tombstone in California claims that she was 111 when she died, but this is probably an exaggeration, as it would require her to have been born in 1758; if she were born in 1758, she would not have needed her parents' signatures on her marriage certificate in 1796!

Malinda and David lived in Barren county, Kentucky until about 1805. About that time they moved to White county, Tennessee, where in 1807 David served as a juror. They resided there until 1817 when David sold his land. They moved to Callaway county, Missouri where David died and a settlement of his estate was made 5 June 1826 listing his wife Malinda and sons William N., John, George C., David H. and Jesse.

In 1854, at the age of about 66, five years after the initial California gold rush, Malinda crossed the plains with an ox team and spent the remainder of her days with her sons John A. and James Townsend Thompson in northern California. She died in Lake county on 29 January 1869, at the age of about 91. Family legend holds that even in her advanced years she remembered the lyrics of her favorite hymns and would sing them during the day.

Her son, James Townsend Thompson, with whom she journeyed to California, served in the Black Hawk War and was a member from Santa Clara county of the third California State Assembly. He died just a few months after Malinda in 1869 in San Diego while on a trip. Malinda and James are burried at Hartley Cemetery in Lakeport, California.
