Narrative

Mary Edith Cooke's life was a picture of both the prosperity, tragedy and opportunity of Victorian England. She was born to a well-off family of a cloth merchant in heavily polluted Manchester, and saw first-hand the terrible human cost of her family's industrial prosperity as her father suffered chronic asthma fron the soot and lint of the city, and her mother died in her early 40s after a four-year battle with a a debilitating cerebral disease that may have been a type of "mill fever." After her mother died, Mary knew the life of a British immigrant, helping to set up a new life with her father and siblings in the British colony of New Zealand, where she would live out her adult life.

Throughout her life, she went by the name "Edith." She was born about 1867 in Manchester, England, during the height of the unbridled industrialization of Victorian England. At the time, Manchester was a thriving and heavily polluted town dedicated to the textile industry, replete with cotton mills that processed cotton from the United States and India. Because of the heavy use of coal to power the manufacture of cloth, the air was heavily sooty, and many of Manchester's inhabitants suffered from asthma. Turbuculosis was also common, and was aggravated by the air pollution.

Edith was listed on the 1881 England census at age 14. She and her siblings living as "borders" in the house of Sophia Armitt in Barton Upon Irwell, Lancashire, England, with a governess. This house may have been a small boarding school. Her parents, Richard Long Cooke and Mary Jane (nee Bridge), did not live with the children. Richard was listed in the same town on the 1881 census. Richard lived separately, although nearby in the same town. Richard suffered from asthma, and Mary Jane suffered from a "cerebral disease" and lived separately at Beech House hospital in nearby Stockport.

As an older child, Edith moved with her father and siblings to New Zealand in 1883. Her father probably moved there to escape the foul air of Manchester. She lived her adult life in New Zealand.

Edith met George Herbert Langdon in New Zealand in Wellington, and had one son by him, John Egerton "Jack" Langdon in 1889. Edith and George later married in 1904 in Wellington. George died young of tuberculosis in 1901, leaving Mary a widow at an early age. Their son, Jack Langdon, later emigrated to the US and then to British Columbia, Canada.

Because her first husband had been a government employee in Wellington, Edith was offered a government job upon his death. She was offered the position of postmistriss of Ross, a small mining town on the west coast. She took this position in 1902. While she lived in Ross, she lived with her father Richard Long Cooke, who died while living with her in 1904. Presumably, her son Jack also lived with them. He left New Zealand in 1919.

In 1905, a young doctor named Thomas Fletcher Telford arrived in Ross to serve as the superintendent of the hospital there. They fell in love, and married on 20 April 1907. She and Tom had a daughter, Annie Edith Beatrice "Bee" Telford, who was born in 1908, 17 years after Mary's first child, Jack, was born. Bee later married Ferrall Moore and moved to live with him in California.

Edith died in 1935, at the age of 69. Tom lived another 35 years until he passed away in 1970.
