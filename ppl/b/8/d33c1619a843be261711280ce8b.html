Narrative

Priscilla Deaver witnessed much of the challenges, successes, and drama of life in the expanding United States during the mid-1800s. She knew a girlhood in the long-established area of the Chesapeake, helped settle a new Midwestern state, felt the loss of her first husband to an early and tragic death, remarried, watched her sons march off to the Civil War, and finally died during the days of railroad expansion two years after the invention of the phonograph.

On Feb. 4,1817 Priscilla was born in Havre de Grace, Maryland, where the Susquehanna River empties into the northern waters of Chesapeake Bay, about 20 miles northeast of Baltimore. February of 1817 was during the last months of the presidency of James Madison, and only two years after the fall of Napoleon. The U.S. was expanding westward, especially into the "northwest" area of upstate New York and Ohio.

Priscilla's father, Richard, seems to have been a farmer who inherited land from his grandfather in Hartford County, Maryland. We don't know when she came to Belmont County, Ohio. There are several Deaver families that seem to come to Belmont at this time. She likely came with one of them, perhaps with her father, before 1832, when the state of Ohio was still being settled.

Priscilla married Michael Moore, a cabinet maker, on 30 August 1832, and shortly after that they had their first child, Erastus Moore, who was born in Belmont. It seems likely that Priscilla and Michael were married in Belmont, too. Michael had been born and raised in the county, and was the grandson of the Michael Moore who came from Pennsylvania and settled on Captina Creek. The younger Michael Moore grew up on the farm of his father, Jacob, in this same area.

Priscilla had four children with Michael, all born in Belmont county. They were Erastus, Adam, Elizabeth, and Michael Emmer. Sadly, just two weeks after their last child was born, Michael died of a morphine overdose in Baltimore. We don't know the circumstances of his death or why he was in Baltimore.

Four years after Michael's untimely death, Priscilla married Eli Mantle, a carpenter, in Belmont on 25 February 1845. They became the parents of three children -- Ellen Jane, Hiram, and Alexander C. The children grew up in Beallsville until the death of the parents. Priscilla and Eli parented all seven children, including the four children Priscilla had with Michael.

The Civil War greatly affected Priscilla, as two of her sons, Adam and Michael, and her son-in-law, Daniel Stuckey, all fought in the war. Of her sons, only the oldest, Erastus, did not fight, probably because when the war broke out he was already the father of two young children, and, with his father dead, Erastus needed to farm the family land. Priscilla must have waited anxiously for their return, and likely paid close attention to the details of the battles during the war. All three of the men in her life who fought in the war returned safely home and lived long lives after the war.

Priscilla was a member of the Belmont Ridge Christian Church, a non-denominational Protestant church that was built by her sons Erastus and Adam. This church was part of the Disciples of Christ organization that would profoundly affect the lives of her children and grandchildren. Three of her grandchildren would become ministers in the Disciples of Christ organization an would help lead congregations throughout the Midwest and plains states. Her son, Adam, bequeathed much of his estate to the church. Priscilla's faith was likely quite strong to have raise such a family of devoted leaders of the church.

Priscilla passed away in Beallsville due to the consumption (likely tuberculosis) on 12 January 1879.
