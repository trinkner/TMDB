Narrative

John Cooke was born about 1802, probably near Manchester, England. At the time, Manchester was a thriving and heavily polluted center of the textile industry. His adult life would have been an event-filled time, as Victorian England experienced tremendous, unbridled industrial growth, and England was rocked by political change and upheaval. In 1832, when John would have been 30, Parliament passed the Great Reform Bill that increased the electoral franchise to include more middle-class voters. This reform was followed by mass working-class unrest as unpropertied laborers demanded equal voting rights. John, as an owner of a cotton mill, likely faced worker demands.

On 13 April 1829, John registered his intention to marry Emma Long in Manchester. Three days later, on 16 April 1829, they officially married. Emma Long, who was born in London, was apparently John's second wife, as the 1829 marriage records indicate that he was widowed at the time of their marriage. Together, they had six children. John and Emma named several of their children with the middle name "Long" which suggestst that Emma's maiden held special significance to them; perhaps it was a family of importance, as all of their children referred to themselves by using their full names, including their "Long" middle names, even in places such as census reports where most of England only used first names and surnames. John and Emma had had their first son, Richard Long Cooke, in about 1831.

John owned a cotton mill in Manchester, England. According to his great-granddaughter, Annie Bee (Telford) Moore, it may have been started by his father. On the 1841 England census, he was listed as a "calico printer" in Layton-with-Warbreck, Lancashire, a small town in Fylde district very near Manchester. Living in Manchester in the 1840s would have been interesting, as Manchester was a hotbed of labor unrest and campaigning for increased suffrange for the working class.

Jocelyn Fish, John's great-granddaughter, suggested in a letter that John may have changed his surname from Colquhoun to Cooke in order to improve his business prospects by removing the Irish or Scottish connotations of Colquhoun. The earliest record of John found so far, the 1841 census, uses the surname Cooke. If he was born Colquhoun, it is possible that he was born in Ireland or Scotland. At the time, many Irish immigrants came to the Manchester area to find work in the fabric mills.

John died before 1861. His wife, Emma, was listed as a widow on the 1861 English census. Four years later, in 1865, she died and her probate document listed that she was a widow and named Richard Long Cooke, her son, to decided the future of her estate. The index for Fylde, the parish of Layton-with-Warbreck, where John and Emma lived in 1841, shows only two John Cookes dying there between 1841 and 1865, both in the first quarter of 1852. John and Emma must have been sufficiently prosperous to justify a probate proceeding to pass their possessions and perhaps land to their son, Richard Long Cooke.
