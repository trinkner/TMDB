Narrative

Eliza Jane Blue was born in Pike County, Alabama about 1853 to Albert G. Blue and Harriet Wynn. Sadly, Harriet seems to have died shortly after Eliza Jane's birth. Albert likely moved the family to Red River, Texas between 1853 and 1856 as he remarried there in 1856 to Samantha Evans. By 1860, Eliza Jane's father had likely died as well. In 1860, seven-year-old Eliza Jane and two of her brothers were living with the Busville family in Red River. In 1870, Eliza Jane lived as a boarder in Girard, Richland Parish, Louisiana, on the same street as three Wynn brothers and their families. Because her father descended from the "Guinea Blue" family of Peter O. Blue, who immigrated to the US from Jura, Scotland, she considered herself of Scottish heritage. She identified herself as a Baptist on the 1881 Canadian census.

By 1874 she had met John Gardiner Telford, an Irish immigrant 10 years her senior who emigrated from Ireland in 1863. They married on 4 March 1876 in Rayville, Richland, Louisiana. We don't know how John came to meet Eliza, or where they met. In any case, Eliza gave birth to their first child, Thomas Fletcher Telford, in 1876 in or near Monroe, Louisiana. It is possible that John met her in Louisiana, perhaps during the aftermath of the Civil War, which offered northerners or immigrants a fair amount of opportunity as the South struggled to rebuild after the war and the emancipation of the slaves. In the late 1860s and early 1870s, northern soldiers helped to garrison the areas of the south under the policies of "Congressional Reconstruction," which divided the southern states into five military districts until these states satisfied the north's requirements for re-entering the union; perhaps John served as a soldier in one such garrison, and met Eliza while he was in the south. Perhaps they met elsewhere, and she returned to Monroe, close to friends, to give birth to her first child.

By 1877, John, Eliza, and their son Thomas, were living in Toronto, Canada. Many British-born families moved to Canada during this time. Whether John and Eliza lived there earlier, or went there only after Thomas was born, we don't currently know. If John had been a northern soldier, and Eliza a southern woman, it may have been convenient for them to leave the South to avoid social pressures. While in Toronto, they had at least three children. The 1881 census listed them living as a household in Toronto. Also, a letter survives that testifies to the 1877 baptism of Thomas Fletcher in Toronto, and the 1881 baptism of Alice, also in Toronto.

Bee Moore, who was John and Eliza's granddaughter, born Annie Edith Beatrice Telford, kept notes that indicate that Eliza died of tuberculosis around 1888 in Arkansas. We don't know how she came to be in Arkansas, nor where John or the children were when she died. Tuberculosis patients often moved south to warmer weather, although they often moved to drier climates than Louisiana or Arkansas. There were tuberculosis sanitariums built in Louisiana, although they mostly were built after 1888 when Eliza died.

Bee's notes indicate that Eliza had friends named the "Wynns" in Louisiana. This must have been a strong friendship, important to Eliza, for this information to survive the years down to Bee. The 1870 census listed Eliza as living next door to a family named Wynn, including a Robert A. Wynn and Joseph Wynn and their households. These same Wynn men appear as witnesses to the marriage of John and Eliza Jane. Perhaps Eliza returned to the South to be with the Wynns when she died. Bee's notes also suggest that Eliza was of Quaker parentage, which is somewhat corroborated by Eliza Jane's declaration of being Baptist and of Scottish heritage, as Quakers were a denomination that originated in the Anabaptist movement.
