Narrative

Johann "Michael" Trinkner lived the quietly successful life of the nineteenth-century American Dream, emigrating from a Germany of dwindling opportunities to the bustling Cleveland of Reconstruction-era America. He lived a reasonably long life, living his first 52 years in Germany, and then living another 12 years after he emigrated to America. When he died, he had created a successful business in the new country, six of his nine children were still living and had married fellow first-generation immigrants in America, and he had prospered sufficiently to leave them a considerable sum of money.

Michael was born the child of a shoemaker, Johann Mattäus Trinkner, and his wife Maria Veronika Weeber, on 16 July 1812. He was the fifth of six children. He was born in Freudental, a small farming village in southern Württemberg, a newly created kingdom whose king had broken away from the Holy Roman Empire only six years earlier to ally with Napoleon who had strongly encouraged the creation of an independent Württemberg. When Michael was born, Napoleon's troops were marching east toward Russia, and would face their wintry defeat outside Moscow when he was just a few months old. Once Napoleon lost power, the Kingdom of Württemberg remained an independent kingdom throughout Michael's life.

As the child of a shoemaker, he probably grew up in humble circumstances. Freudental was, and still is, a tiny village of just a few hundred people, within a quick walk's distance of Erligheim and Löchgau, two other towns where Trinkner families lived. In traditional German style, the villagers lived in the village itself, and the farming men would walk to their neighboring farms by day. Freudental had a church and a school, so likely Michael lived most of his young life exclusively in the village, with occasional ventures to the nearby villages. Given that Michael's formal name was Johann, and that many of his brothers and sons were also formally named Johann, Michael's family was probably protestant.

Although his father had been a shoemaker, Michael became a farmer. When he was 23, he and Philippina Karolina Katharina Keyerleber had a child, a son who died after living just two weeks. One week after their son died, Michael and Karolina married in Freudental on 26 August 1835. They waited another year and a half before having another child, and then they had eight more children in quick succession, their last being born in 1851. With such a large family, they must have lived in fairly humble conditions, given that Michael was a simple farmer.

During Michael's adulthood, Württemberg was independent in both name and alliance, ruled by an absolute monarchy that raised taxes to a level that left little income for farmers and their families. Also during this time, Württemberg farmers divided their lands amongst their many sons, leaving holdings that were too small for the sons to support families. These shifting politics, rising taxes, and land inheritance policies caused many young men to leave for the U.S. once they had finished school. For these reasons, many German families from Wurttemberg began to emigrate to the United States, seeking greater economic opportunity.

Freudental was a very small village, so the success or failure of emigrants in the US would have been known by most of the village. In 1851, Michael's brother-in-law, Friedrich Karl Keyerleber, emigrated to Cleveland, which at the time was the largest German community in America, where he started a successful wagon business. Perhaps because of Friedrich's success, Michael and Karolina's eldest son emigrated to Ohio in 1856. This son, who went by the name John G. Trinkner in America, settled in the immigrant German communities of Cincinnati and Cleveland, working as a barkeep. In May 1859 he sailed back to visit Germany and likely talked with Michael and Karolina about opportunities in Ohio. John returned to Ohio by mid 1860, but it would take another five years for Michael to make the arrangements to move the family to the States. To a father of seven children, including three boys, John's description of Ohio must have been fairly convincing, but Michael did not leave Germany immediately. Perhaps he decided to wait until the US Civil War had ended, or perhaps he was reluctant to leave Freudental while his aging mother still lived. She died in 1864.

In April 1865, Michael petitioned to leave Freudental. Two months later, he and his family settled in Cleveland. Mid-nineteenth-century America would have been both a land of opportunity and one of difficulty for a German immigrant. Nativist political parties arose that campaigned against Irish and German immigrants. The country was rocked by the new equality of the freed slaves and the assassination of a beloved president. The south lay in ruins and required another decade of occupation by northern soldiers before it would be reunited fully with the north. But perhaps within the German community of Cleveland, these national forces may have been felt less by Michael and his family.

The family settled on Kinsman Street in Cleveland. From the records that we have, it is not clear what profession Michael assumed when he arrived, nor how the family made its money. Unfortunately, I have not been able to locate the family in the 1870 census. According to the Cleveland City Directory, a subscription-based annual publication, Michael's middle son Paul worked as an upholsterer by 1866, and continued in this work for a few years. Michael himself is first listed in the 1869 directory as a "farmer" although no record of him owning farm land in Ohio is currently known. Perhaps he listed himself using the profession he had in Germany. By 1871, Michael was listed as running a saloon, aided by his son Paul. In 1871, the family sat for a formal photograph in front of the Michael's "Wein Wirthschaft," a sort of pub and community gathering place popular in nineteenth-century Germany. The fact that Michael probably spoke only a little English and that the sign on the saloon was written entirely in German suggests that this saloon catered heavily to the German community in Cleveland and may have been a place where the immigrants could relax and surround themselves with the culture of the old country. Interestingly, this photo was taken in the very year that Württemberg joined with the Bismarck's Prussian provinces to become the newly unified Germany.

By the early 1870s, all of Michael's children were married, except his youngest son, Charles. Michael's saloon must have prospered, as by the mid 1870s, he had lent a considerable sum of money to four of his children, perhaps to help them start their new married lives or new businesses. The total of these four loans was $5,200 by 1875, which when adjusted for inflation would be worth $97,000 in 2007 dollars. Michael perhaps saved such a large amount of money through selling a farm in Germany, or perhaps through a prosperous saloon business. Certainly the family looks well dressed and well fed in the 1871 photo, which included fancy suits and dresses, as well as gold watch chains for Michael and his son Paul. Sadly, shortly after this photo was taken, Michael's daughter Louisa passed away.

Michael remained close to relatives of his wife's Keyerleber family in Cleveland. F.B. Keyerleber was a witness on Micahel and Caroline's will, and the families lived in the same neighborhood.

Michael waited nine years after arriving in Ohio before declaring his intention to gain citizenship. He and Karolina wrote their will in the mid 1870s, before he died on 21 August 1876. The cause of his death was listed as an "ulcer of the stomach." Karolina lived for another six years, until she died in 1882.

Michael and Karolina's will read as follows:

Last Will and Testament of John M. Trinkner and Caroline Trinkner

Knowing that this Life is uncertain and being of old and of feeble health, but at this writing of sound reason and memory, We herewith dictate and publish our joint last will and testament. I, John M. Trinkner, should I die before my wife, give and bequeath to my beloved wife Caroline all of my real and personal property which I may own or be possessed of in any shape, to be hers during the time of her natural life. And I, Caroline Trinkner, hereby dictate should I die before my husband, I give and bequeath all of my real and personal property or my interest in the same, to my beloved husband, John M. Trinkner, to be his own during the time of his natural life.

After our death it is our will that all of our children shall have equal shares of the value of the Estate remaining except such seems to and shall be deducted from such share as they or any of them has already received, vi John G. Trinkner's share shall be reduced Two Thousand Dollars ($2000.), Elizabeth Dahler's share shall be reduced Fifteen Hundred Dollars ($1500.), Caroline Benz's share shall be reduced Nine Hundred Dollars ($900.) Wilhelmina Nitschke shall have her full share, having as yet received nothing from us. The children of our deceased daughter Louisa Koebler, namely Louisa and Emma Koebler, are to receive their full share of the Estate (that is, the share is to be divided between them equally). Paul Trinkner's share is to be reduced Eight Hundred Dollars ($800.) William Trinkner is to receive his full share of the Estate.

We hereby constitute and appoint Michael Dahler Executor of this our will and testament. Witness our hands + seals this 15th day of August187?
(Signature that reads J. M. Trinkner)
(Signature that reads Caroline Trinkner)

The above will and testament was duly signed sealed and acknowledged by John M. Trinkner and Caroline Trinkner in our presence and at their request and in their presence to have signed our names hereto. (signatures of F. Buchne and F. B. Keyerleber)
