Narrative

Peter Kappes, like the rest of his immediate family, seems to be the classically drawn figure of American immigration in the 19th century. He brought his family from the dwindling opportunity of rural Germany, sailed to America on a cramped steamer, saw his sons go off to the Civil War nearly as soon as they arrived, joined a thriving German immigrant community in bustling Cincinnati, and blended into the hubbub of the postwar, urbanizing economy. We don't know very much, however, about his motivations or successes.

Peter was born around 1814, in the fading years of Napoleon's domination of Europe. Peter's native country, the Grand Duchy of Baden, was immediately on the eastern border of France, and was established through Napoleon's encouragement to become independent of the Holy Roman Empire, which finally ended in 1806. Baden had been crossed by Napoleon's troops, and the land had been devastated by the experience. Peter worked as a farmer, so likely his father, too, had farmed and probably experienced first-hand the loss of crops and deterioration of his land. By 1860, Peter would have been in his 40s, and likely had two sons and two daughters, with his youngest child, Mary, aged 10. His sons were young men, reading to start their own lives. At the time, most farmers divided their land equally among their sons, so Peter's sons stood to inherit only half his land each. Perhaps to offer them better opportunity than he had known, and to escape the threats of disease and warfare that were threatening Germany at the time, Peter chose to move his family to America.

On 15 September 1860, Peter and his family boarded the "Saxonia" in Hamburg to sail to New York. Fighting strong westerly gales throughout the passage, the ship stopped briefly at Southampton, England, and then sailed for two weeks toward New York. The Saxonia carried with her reports of the breaking news of impending Italian unification. As they braced against the wind on deck, the Kappeses and their fellow passengers probably discussed these events between discussions of what kind of life might await them in America. The ship was about 300 feet long and 42 feet wide, with a single funnel smoke stack at its center and three masts that could be rigged with sails. It could carry up to about 600 passengers, 450 of them in third class, where immigrants like the Kappeses probably traveled. They arrived in New York on Tuesday, 3 October. The next day the New York Times wrote of the ship's arrival, and with colorful prose recounted the news of Garibaldi and Victor Emmanuel's exploits in Italy.

October of 1860 was a tumultuous time in America, just one month before Lincoln was elected president and the southern states declared their secession. The first four years for the Kappes family in America were ones of national upheaval and war, which directly touched their lives. After arriving in the U.S., the family settled in Cincinnati where her parents lived the rest of their lives. By 1870, Peter and Barbara were listed in the Cincinatti census, where Peter was listed as a carpenter. Peter later found work as a cabinet maker, a job he held at least through 1890. His son Philip joined the 10th Ohio Infantry, which formed just eight months after the family arrived, and returned a wounded invalid. Family notes suggest that Peter likely had a second son whom the notes do not name; a George Kappes, a few years older than Philip, fought in the 78th Ohio Infantry which was stationed in Cincinnati for a few months in 1862 before seeing action in Tennessee.

By 1880, Peter had seen at least two of his children marry, and he had at least seven grandchildren. Philip, Peter's younger son, had married Elielie around 1868, and had had two children. Philip, an invalid after the war, worked as a chair maker and his entire family lived in the same home with Peter and Barbara in Cincinnati. Peter's youngest daughter, Mary, married another German immigrant, Paul F. Trinkner, in 1868 and moved to Cleveland where Paul's family lived.

We don't know when or where Peter died. He was alive in 1890 for the Cincinnati city directory, but has not been located in the federal censuses after that. When he died, he probably was pleased with the success of the family in joining mainstream working-class American society. Peter's daughter Mary lived until 1920 and successfully raised five of Peter's grandchildren. His son Philip lived only until 1901, but also raised several of Peter's grandchildren.
