Narrative

Lewis J. Mechem saw the founding of Ohio, the War of 1812, the expulsion of Native Americans from Ohio and the Midwest, and the carving of a prosperous farmland from the wooded landscape. He watched his father cut through the woodland to create one of the region's first roads, and attended the first one-room school house in the county. As a young adult, he was profoundly affected by the religious revivalism of the 1840s and founded a church that would determine the lives of his children and grandchildren. By the end of his life, the country had emerged from Reconstruction and the Civil War, and was emerging as an industrialized world power.

In 1804, Lewis was born in Chester County, Pennsylvania. He came to Belmont County, Ohio with his family when he was a boy of four years. His father, John Mechem, brought the family to Ohio in 1808, just five years after Ohio became a state, and lived in various areas of the county before settling on the banks of Captina Creek in 1816. During these first few years, Lewis's father worked with Lewis's uncle and a hired assistant to cut a path through the dense woods between Barnesville and Monroe County. As Lewis grew up, his father worked in a mercantile business. Lewis's mother, born Sarah Evans, was an American-born daughter of Welsh immigrants, and reportedly spoke with a Welsh accent.

Lewis's childhood was likely a stereotypical rural early-nineteenth-century time of farm living, a boy's adventures in the woods, and schooling in a one-room schoolhouse. He attended the first school ever held in Barnesville. When he was just eight years old, the War of 1812 took place, fought in good part because of the conflict over land in Ohio and the other Midwestern states between England and the U.S. The father of Tecumseh, the Shawnee Indian leader who fought on the side of the British against the U.S., was killed not far from Belmont County in south-eastern Ohio. Lewis likely grew up seeing Shawnee Indians in the woods near his home, and also would have known first-hand their disappearance from the Ohio wilderness.

On 1 March 1829, Lewis married Bathsheba Danford, a daughter of William and Elizabeth Danford. Like Lewis, Bathsheba had come to Belmont county as a very young girl when her father moved to the county in search of farmland. Together, Lewis and Bathsheba had 10 children, including one set of twins.

A year later after their marriage, when he was 26, Lewis purchased 160 acres of the land in Belmont County from the federal government during the presidency of Andrew Jackson, when the government was selling land to settlers in Ohio. He and Bathsheba lived and the land as a farming couple, and raised their children.

Lewis's Christian faith was a defining aspect of his life, and would deeply influence the lives of his wife, daughter, and grandsons. During the 1830s and 1840s a Protestant revival swept across America known as the Second Great Awakening. From this movement, which spawned several new Protestant denominations that emphasized the saving grace of Jesus rather than the fire-and-brimstone fear of earlier Calvinist Protestantism, came the non-denominational church organization known as the Disciples of Christ. This group stressed the unity of Protestant Christians, and worked to heal the rifts between the various Protestant groups. Their churches were known by the rather generic name of the "Christian Church." Lewis so firmly believed in the value of this church that founded a new congregation in his house, and then worked with the Belmont community to raise funds and hire carpenters to build a proper church building. The first meeting of the primitive organization was held June 28, 1856, and the church building was completed in 1857 and given the name of the Belmont Ridge Christian Church. Another church building was built in 1885, a few years after Lewis's death. By the time that Lewis died, the church membership was nearly 200 people. This church was built in part by Erastus Moore, a carpenter and neighbor of the Mechems, whom Lewis's daughter, Nancy Ellen, married in 1858. Lewis served as one of the first elders, and his son-in-law, Erastus, served a deacon.

The church profoundly influenced the lives of Lewis and his family. His wife, Bathsheba, dedicated much of her free time to the growth and care of the church and its community, and her obituary described her church work as deeply committed and exemplary. Their daughter, Nancy Ellen, had four sons with Erastus, three of whom became Disciple of Christ ministers. These grandsons of Lewis helped to grow church communities throughout the Midwest and the plains states.

Politically, Lewis was a staunch Whig and later a Republican. As such, he likely was a fervent believer in limiting the extension of slavery in the new territories, and perhaps favored abolition. He was reportedly an influential and representative man of his time and locality.

Lewis died at the advanced age of 82 in 1886 in Belmont county. His wife, Bathsheba, lived five more years, dying in 1891. By the time that he died, he likely had heard music on a phonograph and had grown accustomed to the commonplace rumble of steam-engine trains. His obituary stated that he lived an exemplary life and was highly respected by all who knew him, and that "no doubt he is now enjoying the rest of the saints."
