Leach, William Ephraim

Birth Name Leach, William Ephraim 1a
Gender male
Age at Death 74 years, 7 months, 18 days

Narrative

W. E. Leach was lucky man in that led two lives -- one in Illinois where he was married for 36 years and helped raise eight children, and another in Michigan, where at the age of 71 he married his childhood sweetheart who had remained unmarried since he first wooed her in their youth.

William Ephraim "W.E." Leach was born in 1833 in Sault Ste Marie, Michigan, the oldest city in Michigan, located in the northernmost portion of the state, directly across the St Mary's River from Ontario. He lived as a boy at the army fort where his father Daniel was the commanding officer, a 1st Lieutenant.

By his early adolescence, the family had moved south in Michigan to China Township, where his father took up farming after 14 years in the military. By 1854, when W.E. was 21, he held a position on the China Township school board.

In his early school days in China Township, W.E. met and wooed Drucilla "Dolly" Donihue, a daughter of the aristocratic Virginian John Donihoo, a fervent abolitionist. He refused consent when W.E. proposed marriage to his daughter. Family notes tell of two explanations: one that Donihoo refused because W.E. was a northerner, and another that he refused because W.E. was beneath Dolly's station. Despite the rejection, W.E. remained in love with Dolly the rest of his life.

W.E. left Michigan and moved to Galesburg, Illinois, where his aunt had moved after her marriage. There as a young man he joined the Baptist Church, and was one of the founders of that church. Shortly thereafter, the Civil War started and he enlisted as a volunteer in "Barker's Dragoons," a unit organized in Chicago immediately at the war's start in April 1861. The unit served at Cairo, Illinois and later West Virginia, serving as Major General George B. McClellan's escort. W.E. enlisted for a period of four months, and was discharged in August.

He returned to China Township, where one of his tasks was to serve a school inspector. By 1864, he had moved to Canton, Illinois, where he married Catherine Hipple Martin on 2 July 1864. He lived most of his adult life in Canton, where he and Catherine had 10 children, eight of whom survived. He worked primarily as a clerk for the agricultural office and the Burlington Railroad. Sadly, their last two children, born twins in 1885, died in their infancy.

In 1900, Catherine and W.E. were not living together. W.E. had moved to Neodesha, Kansas, to live with his daughter, Edna, and her husband. Catherine was living in Galesburg with her son William, her daughter Susie, and Susie's husband, Frank Anderson. In her last years, she was in poor health and may have needed her daughter's live-in care, or perhaps Susie and Frank, as newlyweds, could not afford their own home and so lived with Catherine.

We don't know when or why they started living apart, or whether they reunited. The remaining documents let us make a reasonable guess as to when. They were together in May 1885 in Galesburg when their twin children died. We don't know where they lived in 1890 because the 1890 census records burned in a fire. The 1895 Kansas state census does not list W.E., although it does list their daughter, Susan, living in Neodesha with her grandparents, Kate and Hugh Martin. We don't know why Susan left Galesburg and moved to Kansas, but perhaps it was to care for her aged grandparents who were in their 90s. In 1897, together they attended the funeral of Hugh Martin in Neodesha. The June 1900 census is the first document that shows W.E. and Catherine living apart. It lists W.E. in Kansas, living with his daughter Edna and her husband, and unemployed for six months. This suggests that he moved to Kansas six months earlier, perhaps in January 1900, when their youngest child was 18.

With the information we have, we can come to two reasonable possibilities: either they separated because of their feelings for one another, or because of something that was affecting their children.

The first possibility is supported by the 1900 census for Galesburg. Catherine seemed to be living in her own home, perhaps one owned jointly by her and W.E. On the census, she was listed as the head of household, and reported to the census taker that she was a widow. This suggests that the house polled in the census belonged to Catherine, not the son-in-law. That she called herself a widow suggests that she considered herself divorced. In 1900, divorce was still considered shameful or scandalous, so divorced women often reported themselves as widows on the census. With their children grown, maybe W.E. and Catherine felt free to separate. Their marriage was probably truly happy, at least for most of its 36 years, since they stayed together to parent eight children and were known as solid, contributing members of their church and community. It is possible that their marriage ended because of W.E.'s love for his childhood sweetheart, Dolly, or perhaps he and Catherine separated for another reason. The separation possibility seems supported by the census information, but runs counter to the fact that W.E. and Catherine were both devout members of their church and held in high esteem as highly upstanding people in Canton. Family memory does not include a divorce or separation.

The second possibility fits better with family memory, but requires us to interpret the 1900 census report of Catherine as a widow with more skepticism. We know that in 1900 W.E. was living in Kansas with his daughter Edna who had married Lester Stephens. Family memory suggests that Lester might have had problems with alcoholism. Given W.E.'s known devotion to his children, he may have gone to live with Edna and Lester temporarily to help Lester become a stable husband for Edna. Because Catherine seemed to be living in her own home, perhaps jointly owned with W.E., it may be the case that W.E. was simply temporarily absent from their home in Galesburg. This possibility does correlate better to family memory of W.E. and Catherine's standing in the community: it portrays W.E. as a devoted father helping his daughter's marriage, and Catherine as provider of a home to her newly wedded daughter Susan and her husband. This possibility does not explain why Catherine would be listed as a widow. If the census taker had found the home empty and used information from a neighbor to complete the census, he would not have had such perfect date information for Catherine, Susan, Frank, and William H.'s birthdates, so likely Catherine was the person interviewed by the census taker. If so, why would she report herself as a widow?

In short, we need more information to answer the question of why W.E. and Catherine were living separately in 1900.

We don't know whether W.E. stayed in contact with Dolly when he was in Illinois. Her grandnephew had boyhood memories of a man named "Captain" visiting his family in Michigan, bringing oranges for Christmas. According to Suzanne Frantz, whose grandfather recounted this memory, this "Captain" was likely W.E., although this is just one man's memory from when he was a little boy, and might be faulty or refer to a different man. A newspaper article about the marriage wrote that "For over half a century this humble but pretty romance had been gathering its little incidents," suggesting that they did meet occasionally. On the other hand, newspapers also suggested that W.E. and Dolly never met while he was married to Catherine. An East China newspaper reported that their meeting after Catherine's death "was a happy one, as forty-six years had elapsed since either had heard or saw anything of the other."

According to newspapers, W.E. renewed contact with friends in Michigan after Catherine died in 1901, and learned that Dolly was still unmarried. She had stayed in Michigan to care for the children of her brother, Gibbon, and niece, Emma, and for her disabled sister, Matilda. W.E. returned to her and proposed. They married in 1904 in Port Huron, Michigan, at the Baptist parsonage, when he was 71 and she was 74. The newspapers in Michigan heralded the marriage as "one of the prettiest romances in the history of St. Clair County or of Michigan in fact." The papers reported that after the death of his first wife W.E. "found single life oppressive, however, and one day while in a reminiscent mood his thoughts turned to his old love in East China. He immediately entered into a correspondence and learned that she was still single and true to her first love, whereupon he decided to visit her. Naturally the meeting was a happy one, as forty-six years had elapsed since either had heard or saw anything of the other. Mr. Leach returned to Galesburg to get in readiness for the happy event, and he returned to East China a few days ago. The couple, with countenances beaming happily, came to this city yesterday afternoon, secured a marriage license from County Clerk Bartlett, and were made one last evening by Rev. C.D. Gray, of the First Baptist Church."

After the marriage, W.E., a pleasant and courtly old gentleman, was interviewed by a newspaper at the Union Hotel in East China. He said that "We married for company. Dolly and I have known each other for so long and we are happy to be together again. I have eight children, all grown up, by my first wife, and all of the them, I think will be glad to learn of my marriage. We intend to take a little trip and then settle down on Dolly's farm in East China. My, but we are happy."

For four years after their marriage, W.E. and Dolly lived on the Donihoo farmstead on Recor's Point, East China Township, Michigan, that had been settled in 1835 by her parents. He served in various township government positions, until he died in July 1908 at Marine City. Dolly lived four years longer, until 1912, when she died in East China. His obituaries noted that he was a devoted husband and father and true friend.
