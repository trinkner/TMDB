Narrative

Dirk Jan Bent was born 18 Jun 1824 in the small town of Aalten in eastern Netherlands, just a few years after Napoleon's forces left their occupation of the Netherlands and the country resumed its independence. Dirk worked as a laborer, and probably knew substantial poverty. He married Berendina Helmink on 14 March 1851 in Wisch, a smaller town near Aalten. They had had one child before their marriage, their daughter, Grada Berendina, who would go by "Clara" in America. Shortly after their marriage, Berendina gave birth to their second daughter, Berendina, who would go by "Dena" in America. After Dena, they had two more children. Hendrika Gesina died in 1855 at the age of two. A boy, Sevrein, was born in 1855.

When Dirk was 30, his father died. The next year, his mother died. Shortly thereafter, Dirk emigrated with his family from the Netherlands in 1856, leaving from the port city of Rotterdam on the "Epaminondas." They arrived at New York on 26 May 1856, just four years before Lincoln won the presidency and the Civil War erupted. Sadly, en route, their youngest child, Sevrein, died. Since Dirk Jan was a day laborer, they probably traveled in steerage class, leaving them exposed to the elements during the crossing; perhaps the exposed conditions contributed to the boy's death.

By 1860, the family was living in Cleveland, Ohio. Perhaps they chose Cleveland because of the large Germanic community already established there. We don't know whether they spoke Dutch or German, or perhaps both languages. Because his daughter, Berendina, later married Frederick Berger, a German, perhaps the family could speak German. In eastern Netherlands, near the German border, families might be familiar with German.

Dirk married Berendina in the Netherlands when she was already five months pregnant with their first daughter. They left for the United States only five years later. Dirk probably married a second time in 1873, as there is a record of a Derk John Bent marrying Catherine Zenk in Cleveland.

Dirk apparently went both "Derk" and by the anglicized "John" after moving to Cleveland. The 1860 census listed hims as a "laborer," so he probably did follow a specific trade in Cleveland. Perhaps his trade in the Netherlands was not easy to set up in Cleveland, or he lacked money to do so. In the Netherlands, he may have been a farmer, but in Ohio was drawn to the German community in Cleveland and the chance of finding work in the city. According to Roger Trinkner, Dirk worked for the Cleveland Paper Company, the same company where Frederick Berger, who would later marry Dirk's daughter Berendina, also worked.
