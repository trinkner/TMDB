Narrative

Mary Jane Bridge lived a life that was emblematic of the unbridled economic growth of Victorian Manchester in the mid-1800s. She grew up in a modest middle-class home that profited on the growth of the city, she married into a successful family that profited on the growing textile industry, and she at the age of only 45, likely because of the heavy pollution created by the thriving industry that had provided for her family.

Mary Jane was born in 1837 in Ashton-Under-Lyne, a small town east of Manchester and just north of the town of Stockport, which would be her childhood home. Her father was Samuel Bridge, a builder who was sufficiently successful to employ a handful of men. Her mother's name was Nancy. Mary Jane probably knew a childhood of relative comfort, as her father's status of an employer suggests that they were of the middle class.

Manchester at the time was a region famous for its textile industry. In the 1840s, steam power was a new invention to speed the manufacture of cloth, and Manchester was the center of the English textile trade. Unfortunately, this industry polluted the air terribly in Manchester, and the coal soot and lint dust led to widespread asthma and other respiratory ailments. At the same time, tuberculosis was common, and, aggravated by the air pollution, frequently caused early deaths.

In 1865, when Mary Jane was 28, she married Richard Long Cooke, a 34-year-old who had recently left the military after a few years of service with Manchester volunteers. Bee Moore, their granddaughter, remembered that Richard may have inherited his father's cotton mill, and by 1865 both his parents had died, so he likely ran his father's business by the time he and Mary Jane started their family. In any case, Richard worked in the same business as his father, both as a "calico printer" and as a cloth "salesman." Family memory holds that he traveled often, perhaps to Asia to acquire cotton or to sell his fabric. Unfortunately, the air pollution caused by this industry would force him to move away from England, and no doubt worsened Mary Jane's condition with tuberculosis 17 years after their marriage.

Mary Jane and Richard had five children: Mary Edith, George Frederick, Gertrude, Frances, and Henry Philip.

Around 1878, Mary Jane had a photograph taken of her and the children, as well as a portrait taken of her. The photo suggests that the family was reasonably prosperous. Even if the fancy clothes in the photo were borrowed from the photographer, which was often the case for Victorian photo sessions, the fact that they could afford a professional photograph suggests that they were solidly in the middle class. Richard does not appear in the photo, so perhaps it was taken while he was away on business travels and may have been meant as a keepsake for him while he was away.

In the photo, Mary Jane looks to be of robust health. However, within a few months she began suffering from "cerebral disease" and had to move to a hospital, leaving the family in disarray. In Mary Jane's absence, Richard lived separately from his children, but nearby, and needed to board them with a governess to care for them properly. Richard lived with his son Frederick and a servant in Barton Upon Irwell, near Stockport. The other children lived with a governess as boarders in the house of Sophia Armitt also in Barton Upon Irwell.

Mary Jane lived the last four years of her life at Beech House, a Stockport medical facility where she died a year later on 2 August 1882. Her death certificate states that she died of "cerebral disease" and had suffered from it for four years. We do not know what "cerebral disease" exactly meant; it was a catch-all phrase that could have refered to anything from brain cancer, a stroke, cerebral palsy, or dementia. Beatrice Moore's notes indicate that Mary Jane died of tuberculosis, which was a common cause of death in the heavily polluted environment of Manchester. Perhaps Mary Jane suffered from cerebral tuberculosis, a less common form of TB than the more common pulmonary form, but one that can lead to crippling headaches, behavioral changes, and confusion before it finally causes death; symptoms like these may have required her to live in a hospital.

After Mary Jane died, her husband and family moved to New Zealand, in search of cleaner air, where Richard, with his asthma, could breathe more easily.
