Narrative

Gilbert Tompkins's life falls into two clear phases. The first was ruled by the rash passion of a young man in an exciting era, while the second showed the calm planning of a father carving an opportunity out of new lands for his community. As a youth, his passion for American independence led him to enlist in the military three times, and flung him into the stormy chaos of eighteenth-century naval battles from which he ultimately escaped back to Rhode Island only after being impressed as a prisoner into the British navy. After the war and after the death of his first wife, he helped to purchase land in the sparsely settled county of Madison, New York so that the next generation of Little Compton families would have farms.

Gilbert was born in Little Compton, Rhode Island, where he most likely grew up on a farm. He was still living in Little Compton in 1775 when the Revolutionary War began when he was 22. During the war, Gilbert would serve in three tours of duty, two in the army and one in the newly formed marines. His tale of his experiences in the Revolution, especially his final year, makes for a thrilling story. The account here is taken from Gilbert's own sworn testimony to judges in Madison, New York, which he made in 1832 to qualify for the veterans' pension recently passed in Congress. It was also corroborated by other sworn members of the community.

His first tour began in May 1775. He enlisted into the company commanded by two prominent men of Little Compton, company commander Captain Aaron Wilbur and regiment officer Lieutenant Nathaniel Church, whose family would later rescue Gilbert from prison in Canada. They were mustered and then marched to Jamaica Plains near Roxbury, Massachusetts, where they remained some days. From there they marched to Prospect Hill near Boston where they joined a regiment commanded by Colonel Thomas Church and Lieutenant Colonel William Turner Miller. Their brigade was commanded by General Nathaniel Green. They remained stationed at Prospect Hill for the remainder of his eight months of service, the term for which he enlisted, and fought in no actual engagements. When the battle of Bunker Hill took place, the troops Gilbert's regiment remained at Roxbury. Gilbert related that General Washington commanded in person. After Gilbert was discharged on 1 January 1776, he returned to Little Compton.

Shortly after returning home, he enlisted again, either in January or February 1776. He joined a company commanded again by Captain Church, in a regiment commanded by Tivertown's Lieutenant Bailey. They marched to Prospect where they joined a regiment commanded by Colonel William Richmond. They remained stationed at Newport, Rhode Island, until he was discharged from the company in October, 1776. Theirs was the only regiment stationed in Newport at the time, and they spent the season fortifying the place.

A few days after he arrived home, a recruiting officer from the ship "Alfred" that lay in the harbor came to Little Compton looking for volunteers. Gilbert got approval from his officers to enlist as first sergeant of the Marines on board the ship. In a few days he was aboard the Alfred and was mustered. As it turns out, the ship's commander was Captain John Paul Jones, often considered the father of the United States Navy. Gilbert joined a marine regiment commanded Captain Edward R. C. Smith of Providence. They first sailed about 1 November to the coast of Halifax and Newfoundland in company with the sloop Providence of twelve guns, commanded by Captain Harker. While with the sloop they captured three British vessels, including the sloop "Mullish" which carried twelve guns and was destined for Dunkirk loaded amply with supplies and Spanish coinage for payment for the British troops. Soon after this victory, the Alfred was separated from this prize in a gale. On their own, the Alfred captured three more ships in one day when in sight of Newfoundland. They then turned to return to port Boston, but after few days they fell into battle with the British ship "John" from Liverpool and captured the John as a prize. There were few sailors to man both the Alfred and the John, but Gilbert went on board the John, commanded by a man named Saunders.

About the third day after this victory, at night they saw a sail on the horizon behind them. In the morning, Captain Jones signaled for the John to come near to the Alfred so that together they might fight the approaching vessel, which turned out to be the British war frigate, "Milford," a 28-gun warship. But Gilbert's ship could not join the Alfred quickly enough, and were captured on 9 December 1776. As prisoners, they were taken aboard the Milford, which then chased after Captain Jones to Georges Banks, where the Alfred and Jones escaped. The British chained Gilford and his crewmates on the deck where they suffered severely. The Milford carried them as prisoners to the British port of Halifax, where they arrived 25 December. They remained on board the Milford, impressed into the British navy through January.

One day, he was allowed to go on shore when he escaped into Halifax, where he found a job working on board a sloop, and fell in with Thomas Wadsworth who had formerly lived in Little Compton and whose son was on the Alfred. Gilbert somehow secured a pass to go with Wadsworth to his house 50 miles from Halifax, and remained there until spring 1777, when he was taken by a British general and confined in a prison garrison at Windsor as a deserter from the Milford. He remained in the garrison until the commander wrote to Halifax and Gilbert was released to Ephraim Church, also formerly from Little Compton, to be bondsman for him until he had permission to return to the US. He remained in Halifax until the 1 December 1777 when he was released, and started for Rhode Island. He went by land to the Bay of Fundy, where boarded a Salem privateer ship called the "Black Snake" and sailed for Salem. From there he proceeded to Little Compton where he arrived on 27 December 1777.

Although the war continued another four years, Gilbert apparently did not re-enlist after his return. In Little Compton, he married Lucy Brownell, with whom he had one child, Brownell Tompkins. Sadly, Lucy died in 1786. In 1788, when he was 34, he married her younger sister, Mary, who was 17 years younger than he. He lived happily with her for the remaining 46 years of his life, and with her had nine children.

By 1790, he had moved to Westport, Massachusetts, near Little Compton. Twenty years later he would be known as "captain" and would be known to have experience on the sea, so perhaps he started a maritime business there. The "History of Chenango and Madison Counties, New York" makes mention of his working in the "coast trade business."

Gilbert helped create the exodus of Little Compton families to the Madison county area of New York state. In 1792, he visited Madison, New York with Deacon Simmons and Samuel Brownell to purchase land for a Little Compton colony. He bought Lot 84 on the east side of the reservoir, and left his family in Westport for a while to clear off several acres and put up a log house. He had hoped to move his family the next season, but financial obligations kept him in his Westport coast trade business for fifteen more years. Nevertheless, during those years he helped others to settle in Madison by advancing means to purchase homes, and he finally moved his own family from Westport to Madison in 1808 settled on the land he previously selected. He built the house later inherited by his son, Deacon Philip Tompkins, and resided there the rest of his life.

By 1816 he and Mary had joined the First Congregational Church of Madison, the church Mary's parents had joined several years earlier. Gilbert and Mary's son, Samuel, would later marry Lucy Whitcomb, whose family also attended this church. The family was likely quite devout in their faith, as another of their sons, William Brownell Tompkins, became a Congregational minister, and their son Philip became a deacon.

In 1832, Congress passed an act to provide pensions to veterans of the Revolutionary War. In October, Gilbert submitted his paperwork, which at first was denied because he did not have proper exit papers from the military. As a result, he testified before a court in Madison, and brought numerous witnesses before the court to attest to the truth of his story. He was granted a pension in 1834, shortly before he died. His widow, Mary, received his pension until her death in 1844. It is because he had to testify that we have such a rich record of his war experience.

Just as Gilbert had helped to found Madison, New York as a new land of opportunity for land-hungry families of Little Compton, his son Samuel helped to found Galesburg, Illinois in the next generation for the same purpose.
