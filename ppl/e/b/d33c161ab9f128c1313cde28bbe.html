Narrative

Ralph Charles Trinkner was born 8 October 1897 in Cleveland, Ohio. His father, Paul W. Trinkner, worked throughout his life as a pattern maker for a metal foundry. His mother, Catherine, worked as a homemaker, raising Ralph and his two sisters, Elsie and Edna.

For unknown reasons, Ralph left school at age 13 and began to work for the New York Central Railroad company. He started working in the stockyards as a cleaner of boxcars, and then was promoted to assistant foreman and then to foreman. At the end of his career, he served as high-ranking executive of the company, despite never having completed more than an eighth-grade education.

He married Ruth Tompkins in Cleveland. Later, they moved to Toledo, Ohio, where they raised their two children, Jean and Roger. During their time in Toledo, Ralph typically commuted to New York City each week, returning to Toledo by train for the weekends. He and Ruth moved to Rochester, NY, after their son Roger entered college. Ralph and Ruth retired to Laguna Hills, in Southern California, as some of the first retirees to live in "Laguna World," the pioneering Southern Californian retirement community.

Ralph enjoyed smoking cigars, and frequently gave the paper cigar rings to his grandchildren to wear as rings. He had an easy sense of humor and a quick smile.

His son, Roger Trinkner, wrote the following about Ralph:

"Dad dropped out of school after one or two years of high school to work as an office boy at the Collinwood railroad yards in Cleveland, beginning over fifty years employment with the New York Central Railroad. I don't know the reason for his leaving school so young.

"He rapidly advanced within the railroad and was exempted from the draft in WW I at age 20 because of his work status. He worked in the freight division and moved to Toledo, ? Essex street, about 1924. They bought the house I grew up in about 1932; 3812 Maxwell. He became head of the freight division between South Bend, Indiana and Sandusky, Ohio. His office was at Airline Junction( a very large railroad yard in Toledo, one of the largest in the country). I worked there 3 summers( after he had been promoted to New York Headquarters) and saw the awe in which his name was held. I rarely used my last name for when I did there would be sudden total silence as though I had invoked the Lords name. His memory was phenomenal, it was said he knew every part of a box car without looking at a drawing.

"He was a hard worker, meticulous, thorough yet I never knew of his criticizing a fellow worker. He traveled to all parts of the NYC RR after his headquarter promotion but remained in Toledo, probably awaiting my high school graduation. They moved to Eastchester, Westchester County, New York about 1952. This was a northern suburb of New York City,so he had a 40 minute rail commute to his Park Avenue, NYCRR office where he had a personal male secretary. I am sure he was the only employee there without a college degree. While working in New York and living in Toledo, he stayed at the Roger Smith hotel on Lexington Ave. His last years with NYCRR were sad as they were losing business to airlines, trucks and busses, yet were prohibited from owning any of these other transports. Unions wreaked havoc when steam was replaced by diesel engines. NYCRR merged with their arch competitor, the Pennsylvania RR, then closed the passenger division completely. The CEO was a scoundrel named Pearlman who talked a good story and strongly urged all top management to buy NYC stock as a show of good faith in the company, at the same time he was selling all his stock. So dad lost a lot of his savings. He and Ruth retired to Leisure World in Laguna Beach, California in 1964.

"He loved baseball and always had a game on the radio. His favorite player was Napoleon Lajoie, second baseman for the Cleveland Indians 1910-20. He enjoyed playing cards which they did most Saturday nights, especially canasta. He rarely lost, I suspect he could count cards, as it was very rare to beat him in gin rummy. He always was in good humor unless President Roosevelt's name was brought up, but Evan then I never heard him swear or use vulgar language. (During WWII, Roosevelt went to Maine on the railroad for some secret meeting and dad was in charge of the logistics. They did not meet, but dad saw him as he looked through a car window). Dad was a 32nd-degree Mason in Cleveland and was the youngest man in Ohio to ever reach that level . He never participated in Masonic in events in Toledo that I saw, however.

"Dad always wore a tie and vest, white shirt with detachable collar, and a hat. After consulting his doctor in Toledo (Dr. Wagoner, a gruff, dictatorial, German type) he was told to sleep in Sunday mornings as his 1500-mile weekend commuting was too tiring. So, mother and I would attend church, and Dad would sleep in on Sundays.

"I recall Dad as a very capable man, probably had a photographic memory, who led by example rather then by words, who had very high expectations for himself and was very well liked by all who knew him. He had an empathy for the little guy, underdog, youngster as he always rooted for that type of player when we went to the watch the Mud Hens play in Toledo. He was totally devoted to his family, providing everything he could for his kids. On commenting on someone's behavior, one of his favorite expression was that so and so "conducted himself" well or poorly. He moved to our house during the last year of his life when metastatic prostate cancer was diagnosed and he underwent radiation treatment at Peninsula Hosp. He moved to Carlmont Convalescent Hospital in Belmont, California, for the last few months of his life. Typically, he never complained, even when suffering considerable pain."

Ralph's daughter, Jean (Trinkner) Hewitt, wrote the following about Ralph:

"I was born in East Cleveland in 1925 and we moved to Toledo when I was about four in 1928. We rented a house on Essex and one on Rushland before buying on Maxwell in 1930 or 1931, right after the banks closed at the beginning of the Depression. I remember them being concerned about paying for drapes, carpet etc. because they couldn't get funds. In 1939 Dad was promoted to NYC but traveled all the time. We would pick him up Friday evening at the train station and take him to the station Sunday evening. I don't remember his title at that time.

"He was promoted to Assistant to the Vice President of the Freight division and transferred to NYC full time in 1952. We had just moved to Connecticut which was only 45 minute drive to Westchester so we saw them a great deal.

"No question, Dad was a wonderful, caring, sensitive, man with a great sense of humor and infectious laugh. Although he didn't have a "formal education" he was very intelligent. I think he didn't finish high school for financial reasons. When my husband, John Hewitt, first started making HO-gauge trains Dad enjoyed explaining all about the different cars etc. but only when asked.

"He liked little children and really enjoyed his grand children.

"Dad was an avid baseball fan and changed his allegiance from his favorite Cleveland Indians to the Yankees. As a very young man he played semipro baseball as a catcher."
