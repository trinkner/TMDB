Narrative

Piety, temperance, and reformed education - all ideals of the radical Galesburg of the 1840s. In this hotbed of progressive Protestant ideals, Franklin Tompkins grew up largely in the shadow of his father, and lived there for most of his life. In 1835 when Franklin was just 13, his father helped organize the purchase of land for the city as an experimental community centered on a model college, true to the idealistic vision of George Washington Gale, a Presbyterian minister from New York state who dreamed of establishing a college in keeping with his belief in an elevated form of manual labor. Franklin, as a young man worked as a teacher in this idealistic educational experiment, perhaps striving to match the high academic ideals of the age, but soon turned to carpentry and apparently lived most of his life in the traditional style of a Midwestern Protestant family, likely taking professional advantage from the need for new buildings as the population of Galesburg swelled.

Franklin was born to Samuel and Lucy Tompkins and on 15 November 1821 in Hamilton, Cayuga, New York., just as the United States population was expanding westward past the Mississippi into the newly formed states of Illinois and Missouri. His father, Samuel, was a founder of Galesburg who traveled from New York state with Gale. Samuel joined the land purchasing committee, and traveled from Hamilton, New York, to the prairie south of Knoxville to acquire land for the new college and community. After purchasing the land, Samuel served as a surveyor for the new town, which included "Tompkins" street as a prominent boulevard. The first 25 settlers arrived in 1836 and built temporary cabins in Log City near current Lake Storey, just north of Galesburg, having decided that no log cabins were to be built inside the town limits. The town of Franklin's adolescence was home to the first anti-slavery society in Illinois, founded in 1837, and was a stop on the Underground Railroad. The city was the site of the fifth Lincoln-Douglas debate, on a temporary speaker's platform attached to Knox College's Old Main building on October 7, 1858. Although we don't know for sure, it is likely that Franklin and his father witnessed this debate. As the country inched towards civil war, Galesburg was considered one of the nation's capitals of abolitionism and a supporter of Lincoln's new Republican party.

On 24 June 1851, when he was 30, Franklin married the 20-year-old Samantha A. Ward in nearby Berwick, Illinois, her home town. She was the daughter of John G. Ward, a farmer and Baptist preacher who had purchased land in Berwick in the 1830s and moved the family from Pennsylvania. Likely, Franklin and Samantha had moved to Illinois within a few years of each other - he from New York as a young adolescent, and she from Pennsylvania as a very young girl. Between 1859 and 1871 they had four children.

Apparently, the Civil War little affected Franklin or his immediate family. When the war broke out in 1861, he was 39 and the father of a young child and a wife pregnant with their second child.

By 1880, Franklin was working as a "Master Carpenter," perhaps employing other carpenters. His health, though, may have been deteriorating, as family memory holds that he was ill towards the end of his life and perhaps not able to work full time. In 1880, his children were working to help support the family. His son, William, was 21 and working as a "depot caller." Cora, 18, worked as a dressmaker. His younger son, Henry, was 16 and working as a bookkeeper, after having worked for a year or two as a Western Union telegram boy. Only Mary, 9, was still in school.

The final year of Franklin's life, unfortunately seems to have been tainted by family disagreement. His 19-year-old son Henry had fallen in love with a young Katherine Leach, who was pregnant with Henry's child. Both Henry and Katherine were under 21, and so needed their parents' permission to marry. For reasons we don't currently know, Franklin refused to give his consent, despite the lengthening of Katherine's pregnancy and her approaching due date. Franklin died on 22 April 1885 in the ninth month of Katherine's pregnancy. On that same day Henry's mother gave her consent for the marriage to go forward, and Henry and Katherine wed. That same day Katherine gave birth to a son.

By the time of Franklin's death, the population was just over 12,000. Likely Franklin had built many of the houses or furniture for the new residents, and had helped to make his father's shared dream of a new town on the prairies a reality. His son, Henry, would go on to become a successful railroad executive, as Galesburg became an important stop on two of the century's new railroads, the Chicago, Burlington & Quincy Railroad, and later, the Santa Fe Railroad.
