Narrative

Ruth Tompkins was born prematurely, and was so small at birth that the doctor did not give her a birth certificate, because he believed she would die shortly. Her mother took her home and put her in a cigar box, which her grandmother tended by the fire, pinching the little Ruth periodically to keep her awake and moving. Ruth lived, and grew to be a tall woman, even taller than her mother.

From Roger Trinkner, 2007: "Ruth Elizabeth Tompkins born in Galesburg, Illinois, and moved to Buffalo, New York, as a girl. She and her family moved to East Cleveland when she was about 15. She attended the Baptist church in East Cleveland, where John D. Rockefeller Sr., the founder of Standard Oil and wealthiest man in America, also attended. He gave dimes to kids in Sunday school, including Ruth. I think she finished high school but am not positive.
She had a fine singing voice and took voice lessons with a well known voice teacher who also coached John Charles Thomas and Nelson Eddy with whom Ruth would sing duets when they visited Cleveland.
She met her future husband, Ralph, at church functions, married, and moved to Toledo about 1928. Her first child, Jean, was born in 1925. Their second daughter, born about 1929, died of pneumonia 1931. I (Roger) was born in 1933.
Ruth was devoted to her family, encouraging in school work, but not overbearing. During my grade school, the proper etiquette required inviting the school principal and the specific grade teacher to lunch at the student's home at the beginning of each school year. This she did without fail, but with considerable unease to me. She objected to my playing football in high school, which I quit as a freshman, although not really because of her wishes. She made sure I was scrubbed and well dressed, with a freshly ironed shirt every day for school. The first impression my college pre-med dean had of me, was the kid in the crisply ironed shirt. His opinion was crucial in being admitted to medical school.
Ralph and Ruth delayed moving to New York until I finished high school, even though it meant she would be alone during the week as dad traveled to New York. Ruth excelled in all she did, belonged to several church groups, the PEO, and others I don't recall the names of. She invariably became president of each. Ruth attended the Ashland Avenue Baptist Church, and practiced her religion completely; she never drank alcohol, swore, spoke ill of others, or went to movies on Sunday. Yet she did not impose her views on others.
We always had a dog, probably to keep her company while dad traveled and because I always seemed to find a stray dog on my paper route. She died of uterine cancer which was not told to her by her surgeon after the second operation. So it fell to me to tell her -- news which she took with a slight smile and nod of her head. She was an A+ mother and wife."
