Narrative

The life of Friedrich "Paul" Trinkner was emblematic of the struggles and successes of German immigrants in the late 1800s. As a young adult, he fled the hardship of poverty and dwindling opportunity in rural Germany, and worked in a series of family businesses in Cleveland, only to die at the age of 42 of tuberculosis. He experienced the excitement and anxiety of a nineteenth-century sea voyage to a foreign country, swam the rough currents of urban free enterprise, watched his children attend American schools and learn English, and finally died of a disease that ravaged the immigrant classes of American cities. Before he died, he probably felt satisfaction in having provided a better opportunity for his children's success than he had had growing up in his rural German village.

Paul was born in 1847 in south-western Germany in the small farming village of Freudental where many families of the Trinkner name lived. His given name was "Freidrich Paul," but he went by his middle name, "Paul," as was the custom for Germans of the time. Most Trinkner families there worked in agriculture, usually as wine gardeners or farmers. Paul's father, Johann "Michael" Trinkner, was not listed as a wine gardener, but as a farmer in the Freudental records. He probably farmed grains, perhaps wheat or barley. Paul grew up in Michael's household, the seventh of nine children.

In the 1840s, Freudental provided school for children, and Paul probably attended it. The village was set up like most other German rural towns, with a village center of closely built homes, a few stores, a church, and a town square. Farms surrounded the town, and the farmers lived close together in the village, walking to their individual farms. Freudental was in the Kingdom of Württemberg, near the region's main city of Stuttgart. Trinkners had lived in the area since the end of the Thirty Years War in 1647, when David Trinkner had moved there from Lower Saxony and fathered a line of descendants who would contribute to the populations of the three small villages of Freudental, Besigheim, and Löchgau, all within a mile's walk of each other. These Trinkner families must have known each other well and seen each other often. Later, in Cleveland, the Trinkners would live very close to each other too, and establish a family business.

By the time Paul was born in 1847, the Kingdom of Württemberg had become independent from the Holy Roman Empire. In 1806 Napoleon had exerted influence in the region and encouraged the formation of the independent kingdom closely allied with France. Napoleon's troops trampled the region as they marched through Württemberg to battles against Austria and Prussia. Following Napoleon's fall in 1812, the kingdom became independent in both name and alliance, ruled by an absolute monarchy that raised taxes to a level that left little income for farmers and their families. Also at this time, in Württemberg farmers divided their lands amongst their many sons, leaving holdings that were too small for the sons to support families. The result of the shifting politics, wars, rising taxes, and land inheritance policies was that whole school classes immigrated to the U.S. immediately after they had finished school.

Paul's brother Johann Gottlieb was ten years older and the first of the family to venture to America. In 1856, when Paul was nine, "John" finished school and left Freudental for the U.S. He found work in Cleveland and lived there for three years before returning to visit Freudental in 1859; after visiting Freudental, John returned to America, and settled in Cincinnati. Six years later, when Paul was 18, his father, mother, brothers, and sisters left Freudental for the United States, probably persuaded by John's description of the opportunity in the growing German community that awaited in Ohio. They entered the U.S. in June 1865, and quickly settled in Cleveland. John Gottliem continued to live in Cincinnati until 1870, so the family may have gone to Cincinnati for a few months before settling in Cleveland.

Once in the U.S., Paul consistently called himself "Paul Trinkner" which reflected the German tradition of using what Americans would call the middle name as the primary name. Although born "Freidrich Paul Trinkner" he was known as "Paul" by friends and family. In the U.S., his brother, Johann Gottlieb, went by "John G." and his father Johann Michael went by "Michael."

Settling in Cleveland during the tumultuous months immediately after the end of the Civil War and the assassination of Lincoln, Paul and his family quickly found friends from the Freudental area, including Michael Dahler, a man six years older than Paul who married Paul's sister, Elizabeth. Dahler and Elizabeth had had a child in Freudental in 1864, and later had more children in Cleveland. Dahler had come to Cleveland by 1859 and established a boarding house and saloon, where Paul lived when he first arrived in Cleveland.

The finances of the family do not seem to have been desperate, but neither was the family rich. Michael, Paul's father, saved a considerable amount of money for a German farmer, which probably indicates that he sold his land in Freudental at a good price. His will indicated that by the time of his death he had already given away to his children $5,200, which when adjusted for inflation would represent approximately $76,000 in 2006 dollars. His will suggested that more money would be given to the children after he and their mother died. Most of this money must have come from selling land in Freudental, but in 1871 Michael was listed in the city directory as owning a saloon. A family portrait from 1871 shows this saloon as a "Wein Wirthschaft" owned by "M. Trinkner." A wirthschaft was a uniquely German type of saloon that offered dances, wine and beer, and a general place for the local German community to relax together. Perhaps this saloon was a thriving business and helped build his savings. John Gottlieb received the bulk of the advances, receiving $2,000, which he might have to purchase a hotel in Cleveland.

Paul never established a long-term business and seemed to be a jack of all trades. Upon arriving in Cleveland, he quickly entered the business of making furniture. By 1871, he was working at Trinkner Brothers & Company furniture store on Woodland Avenue with his brother William and sister-in-law Josephine, John's wife. Michael's will indicates that he gave Paul $800, which perhaps allowed Paul to help start this business. In the 1880 census, he was listed as an "upholsterer." He also he worked as a bartender and in a beer garden with his brother John. Finally, he worked as a harness maker. None of these occupations apparently lasted more than two years

Within the first few years of living in Ohio, Paul met and married Mary Kappes, who was born officially as Margaretha Maria Kappes. She had been born in the small village of Wertheim on the Main river in the Grand Duchy of Baden, the Germanic state west of Württemberg on the French border. She had come to the U.S. in 1860 as a ten-year-old girl, five years before Paul. Her family settled in Cincinnati, where her father, Peter, worked as a cabinet maker. She and Paul may have met in either Cleveland or Cincinnati, possibly through John Gottlieb, who lived in Cincinnati until 1870 when he moved to Cleveland. Perhaps because her father and Paul worked in the furniture business, they met through their work.

Paul and Mary likely spoke German in their home, and lived on the Kinsman Road in Cleveland, the same street where Michael and John lived. Paul and Mary had seven children, starting about 1870 when their first child, Emaline, was born. Although their fifth child, Ella, died when she was three, their other children lived to adulthood.

Paul died of tuberculosis in Cleveland on 8 January 1890, at the young age of 42. At the time, tuberculosis was rampant in the city, causing at least ten percent of the deaths across the city each year. When he died, his youngest daughter, Elsie, was only six and his oldest child, Emaline, was 20. At his death, he had reason to be proud of his accomplishments as a first-generation immigrant in integrating his family into American society and providing for their future. His children, all born in the U.S., had attended school, likely spoke English as well or better than they spoke German, and would succeed in raising families of far greater material wealth and health than he himself had known.
