Narrative

The story of Eda Thompson's life is one of happy devotion to a faith and a man, that took her to live in many states, from Missouri to the newly settled southwest, the western plains, and finally to New England. Her life was buoyed by the new spirit of Protestant fervor that swept the Midwest during the early 1900s and led to Prohibition.

Eda Penick Thompson, daughter of Civil War Lt. Colonel Philip Austin Thompson and Susan Thompson, was born in Langdon, Missouri on April 16, 1873 where she spent the early years of her life. Her father named her Eda Penick in memory of General W.R. Penick, the officer under whom he served during the Civil War. Her early life was one of privilege and wealth, as her father had prospered as a merchant, farmer, banker, state senator, and high-ranking officer. Their house was one of the largest in the county, and was filled with children and servants. The house today is listed on the National Register of Historic Places.

Like her brothers and sisters, she was raised in the Christian Church of the Disciples of Christ, and grew up attending the church near Langdon which her maternal grandfather, Dr. Richard Buckham, and her father had helped to found. The Disciples of Christ originated in the Restorationism movement of the early 1800s that strove to downplay the split of the Christianity into separate denominations. Unfortunately, this church, too, fell victim to such splits in 1906. Her devout faith and membership in the Disciples of Christ church would affect virtually every aspect of her life, including her choice of husband, her profession, and how she raised her children.

She completed her high school education at Rock Port, Missouri, after which she attended the State Teachers College at Kirksville, Missouri. Following this she engaged in special study in the east. For an number of years she was engaged in teaching, most of this time being spent in the public schools of Tarkio, Missouri, where the family had come to make their home.

On June 20, 1899, she married Zwinglius Moore, who was at that time pastor of the Christian Church of the Disciples of Christ in Tarkio. Immediately following, they moved to Albany, Missouri, where Zwinglius worked as president of the Central Christian College and served as pastor of the Christian Church there. She and Zwinglius had three children, only one of whom survived, as their first and third children, both daughters, died in infancy. Ferrall Harman Moore, their only surviving son, was born in 1904 in Taylorville, Illinois.

For most of his adult life, her husband suffered from cardiac asthma, which increasingly required him to live in a dry climate. As a result, between 1900 and 1917 Eda and Zwinglius served several Disciple of Christ churches in the drier western plains states, living in Paris, Missouri; Milford, Illinois; Princeton, Missouri; Taylorville, Illinois, Fort Morgan, Colorado; Deming, New Mexico; and Hoisington, Kansas. Eda was also highly active with the Christian Woman's Board of Missions, a grassroots organization intended to serve women and children worldwide. While many members also supported other 19th century causes such as temperance and suffrage, CWBM was the 'grand passion' in the lives of many nineteenth- and twentieth-century Christian Church women.

Zwinglius died of his asthma in May, 1917, in Hoisington. Throughout their lives, Eda and Zwinglius made do on a humble pastor's salary, yet provided a family atmosphere of warmth, integrity, and caring. Their son, Ferrall, wrote a beautiful vignette about their last Christmas, in 1916, which portrayed the family as one of exceptional love and companionship. Also, Zwinglius's father, Erastus, wrote a a wonderfully detailed journal of his travels to Deming, New Mexico, to visit his Eda and her family.

After her husband's death, Eda became Field Secretary for the United Christian Missionary Society, a not-for-profit Disciples of Christ evangelical organization started in 1920 in a merger of The American Christian Missionary Society, The Christian Woman's Board of Mission, and The Foreign Christian Missionary Society. Her duties called her to all parts of the country, though much of her time was spent in New York and the New England states. Her work consisted largely of organizing schools for missionary education. Her small leather-bound New Testament, published by the Pocket Testament League, survives to today -- likely one very similar to the Testaments that she gave to people during her evangelical activities. Inside its front cover is her signature, and toward the back is a confession of faith signed by her son, Ferrall.

When her sister in 1924, Eda moved to Tarkio to care for her mother, Susan Thompson. Eda had been in failing health for the past three years of her life, and shortly before her death she suffered a partial stroke of paralysis. She died on 27 June 1926, at her mother's home, just having returned from a hospital in Omaha where she had been under the care of a specialist who had diagnosed her illness as "pernicious anemia." Her dedication to her church and religious devotion left a profound effect on her son, Ferrall, who remained steadfast in his faith throughout his life. Her passing was sincerely mourned in the local newspapers.
