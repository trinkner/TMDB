Narrative

For Erastus Moore, life in the mid to late 1800s in rural, southeast Ohio was largely stable, agricultural, religious, and in keeping with the stereotypical images of small-town pre-industrial America. Belmont County seems to have been affected only little by the national events of the time. Although many of the state's sons fought in the Civil War, only two of the war's lesser battles took place in Ohio, and none were in Belmont. Although the northern cities like Cleveland and Toledo were heavily affected by the coming of the railroad, industrialization, and European immigration, these dramatic agents of change affected Belmont County much less. In short, Erastus's life was dedicated to his family, his church, and his farm -- all of whose pursuits seem to have been successful. His farm would provide for his family throughout his life, three of his sons would become ministers, and he and his wife would help found and nurture a long-lasting church community. His 1910 memoir of a trip to visit his sons showed that he enjoyed life and was a happy man in his old age, proud of his son's accomplishments.

On 25 October 1833, Erastus was born the eldest of Michael and Priscilla Moore's four children. His father, Michael, a cabinet maker, died when Erastus was only seven of an overdose of morphine in Baltimore. The circumstances of his father's overdose isn't explained in family notes. Erastus's mother married Eli Mantle and had three more children, making for a family of seven children over all. The family lived in Wayne Township of Belmont County in southeast Ohio.

He grew up in the old home where he was born. As a young man, he engaged in carpentry and later bought what was known as the George Dawson farm, where he lived throughout his life until he moved to live with his daughter in nearby Beallsville when he was in his 80s.

In his boyhood he united with the Christian church, in which he always took an active part. He was one of the Charter members of the Belmont Ridge Christian Church, organized in 1856.

In the early days of Belmont County, there were only four churches: the Quaker meeting house, the Christian Church, the Methodist Episcopal Church, and the Belmont Ridge Christian Church. As a boy, Erastus was a member of the Christian Church, in which he played an active role throughout his life. The organization to which this church belonged, the Disciples of Christ, originated in the Restorationism movement of the early 1800s that strove to downplay the split of the Christianity into separate denominations.

He was a founding member of the Belmont Ridge Christian Church, and provided his carpentry skills to help construct the church building. A.T. McKenley, in his Centennial History of Belmont County, mentioned that "the carpenters being Erastus and A.T. Moore" (A.T was Erastus's brother, Adam). A document is on file of the agreement between Erastus and the building committee of the Church of Belmont wherein E. Moore agreed to build the Belmont Meeting House. The agreement was explicit as to what was to be built. The amount to be paid was $250 and the contract was dated 1 September 1857. The church was erected in 1858. Its first meetings were held in the home of his father-in-law, Lewis Mechem, who was instrumental in organizing the church. In 1873 Erastus was ordained to the Eldership of this church, and he held this office to the end of his life.

On August 1, 1858, he married Nancy Ellen Mechem, the daughter of Lewis Mechem. Although Belmont was a small town and the two no doubt knew each other as children, Erastus and Nancy likely grew closer because of their mutual interest in the church. They seem to have gotten married just after the church was built, so they probably courted while Erastus was building it. The meetings and worship of the Christian Church community took place in the home of Lewis Mechem before the formal church was built, so Erastus probably spent some time with Nancy at these meetings. When they married, he was 25 and she was 20. They had seven children, including four sons and three daughters.

The names of their sons perhaps indicates much about Erastus and Nancy and their times. Three of their sons were named for Protestant reformers: Melanchthon, Zuinglius, and Luther. All three of these sons followed their namesakes and became Protestant ministers. Their fourth son they named Edison in 1878, one year after Thomas Edison invented the phonograph. This son, too, followed his namesake and went into business.

In 1860, Erastus and Nancy moved to a new farm in section 2 of Wayne Township, Belmont county. The Centennial History of Belmont County refered to this as a "fine farm."

In 1909, after 51 years of marriage with Erastus, Nancy died. She was well loved by their community, and no doubt sadly missed by her husband. In 1910, just after her death, Erastus embarked on a tour by train and horse-drawn coach of the western plains states to visit his three minister sons, Zwinglius in New Mexico, Melanchthon in Kansas, and Luther in Ohio. All three were ministers in the Disciples of Christ organization. Erastus's journal of the trip survives. In the journal he recorded several agricultural observations, such as soil depth and crop height, as would be expected of a life-long farmer. He also wrote of his three sons with immense pride.

Erastus apparently worked as a farmer until late in life, as the 1910 census listed his as an active "farmer." He died on 19 February 1922 in Beallsville, Ohio.
