Narrative

Bathsheba Moore was one of the first generation to be born in Belmont County and was the child of two of the oldest pioneering families in the county. She would have been four years old during the War of 1812 which saw fighting between the Americans and English and English-allied Native Americans in Ohio. She also saw the expansion of the economy away from the river traffic of the nearby Ohio river to railroads that would make the cities of Toledo and Cleveland of upstate Ohio the center of trade. Most of all, hers was a rural life that was strongly inluenced by the growth of protestant fervor in the 1830s.

Bathseba Danford was born in Washington township, Belmont County, Ohio, in 1808, where she was raised and ever afterwards lived. Her father came from Pennsylvania in 1797, accompanied by his two brothers, Ambrose and Samuel Danford. Bathsheba's mother was Elizabeth Moore, daughter of Michael Moore. Bathsheba had nine brothers and sisters.

Bathsheba married Lewis Mechem in 1829, and they reared a family of ten children. Lewis worked throughout his life as a farmer, and came to be a respected central person of the community. His farm neighbored farms owned by the Moore family, including that of Erastus Moore, who would marry Lewis and Bathsheba's daughter, Nancy.

Lewis and Bathsheba were dedicated members of the Christian Church, a non-denominational protestant church that was affiliated with the Disciples of Christ. During the early 1800s, the protestant revival movement known as the "Second Great Awakening" traveled quickly across the United States, spreading a new protestant version of Christianity that emphasized forgiveness and the availability of salvation for everybody through grace; the Disciples of Christ were such a faith. Lewis and Bathsheba were the founders of the Christian Church at Belmont Ridge, starting the church in their own home, until they were able to build a meeting house. The new church was built by Erastus and Adam Moore, also lifelong residents of Belmont county. Just after the new church building was finished, Erastus married Lewis and Bathsheba's daughter, Nancy. Bathsheba was a member of the church for over 60 years. Her obituary stated that "with tireless hands she continually lingered beneath the shadow of the cross. She was especially kind to the poor, and no tramp ever went from her door unfed." Bathsheba would have been proud of three of her grandsons -- children of Nancy and Erastus -- who became ministers for the Disciples of Christ's Christian Church.

Bathsheba died in 1890, at the age of 82.
