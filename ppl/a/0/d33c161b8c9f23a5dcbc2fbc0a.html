Narrative

Paul W. Trinkner was born in Cleveland in 1872, and lived the rest of his life in Cleveland. He was the son German immigrants who had arrived in the United States in the middle of the 1800s. His father, Paul F. Trinkner, came from Freudental, Germany in 1862, and his mother, Mary Kappes, came from Baden as a young woman. Paul likely grew up speaking a fair amount of German in his home.

Paul married Katheryn Berger in Cleveland in 1895. She was the daughter of German and Dutch immigrants who had arrived in the United States in the middle of the 1800s. Her father, Frederick W. Berger, came from Berlin in 1862, and her mother, Berendina Dent, came from the Netherlands in 1856. We don't know which language she spoke with her parents in their home.

Paul worked throughout his life as a "pattern maker" in metal foundries. He was listed with this profession in every census between 1900 and 1920, as well as in the Cleveland City Directory. He must have started working as a pattern maker by the time he was 28, possibly attending technical training school in his late teens, and serving as an apprentice for a few years around 1890. In the early 1900s, foundries cast metal objects by pouring molten metal into molds, usually made of sand. To make a mold, foundries needed a pattern, which was ordinarily a full sized model of the object to be cast. Making the pattern was the work of the "pattern maker," often referred to as the aristocrat of the foundry. Pattern-making was usually a woodworking trade, as most patterns were made of wood, metal being used only when a large number of castings are required from a single pattern. Pattern-making was a highly skilled occupation, requiring technical knowledge as well as mechanical skill. Besides being able to perform the most complicated woodworking operations, the pattern maker required a knowledge of foundry work, drafting, shop mathematics and machine-shop practice. Typically a pattern maker had some training in a technical or trade school and then worked as an apprentice or helper. Good pattern makers were scarce, and so pattern makers usually found work easily. Although pattern making did not demand as much physical exertion as other jobs in the metal trade, working in foundries was strenuous and hard, and required on the part of the worker a strong physique, even for pattern makers. Frequently the hours were long, for blast furnaces and steel mills were operated 24 a day. The workers were often exposed to intense heat, and were obliged to handle heavy loads.

Paul must have been successful in this trade, as he worked in it throughout his life. His income, though, might not have been dependable. He and Kate did own their own home and had a mortgage, according to the 1900 and 1910 censuses; however, Ralph, their son, did leave school before graduating from high school. Allthough the reason for him leaving school isn't known for certain, family memory holds that he left to help support the family.

Kathryn kept home and raised their three children, Ralph Charles, Elsie, and Edna. In 1900 Paul and Kathryn lived on Bower Street in Cleveland, next door to Kathryn's sister and her husband, Amelia and John Graul. Both Amelia and John were also children of German immigrants.

Paul died in Cleveland when he was 58, in 1930.
