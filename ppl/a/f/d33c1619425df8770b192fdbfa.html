Narrative

Zwinglius Moore was born in Belmont county, Ohio, January 27, 1862 to Erastus and Nancy Ellen Moore. His father, a successful farmer and one of the early settlers in Belmont county, helped to found the county's first church organized by the Disciples of Christ, named the "Christian Church." The family was devoted to their faith, as three of Erastus's sons became ministers in the Diciples of Christ organization. This organization originated in the Restorationism movement of the early 1800s that strove to downplay the split of the Christianity into separate denominations. Unfortunately, this church, too, fell victim to such splits in 1906.

Like his brother, Melanchthon, Zwinglius attended Bethany College, a small liberal arts college in West Virginia that was closely associated with the Disciples of Christ. He graduated in 1894, and began his career as a Disciples of Christ minister in Michigan. From 1998 to 1900, he was president of the Central Christian College at Albany, Missouri, and served as pastor there at the same time.

On 20 June 1899, while the college president, he married Eda Thompson, the daughter of Lt. Colonel Philip A. Thompson, who was a Missiouri state senator and leader in the anti-guerrilla fighting in southern Missouri during the Civil War. Their wedding, and the reception which took place at Eda's mother's house, was described as follows in the Central Christian Collegian, a publication for the college community. "The crowning social affair of the college year was held at the home of Mrs. Susan Thompson, Tarkio, Mo., June 20. It was the marriage of her daughter. Miss Eda F., to our beloved President Zuinglius Moore. Each guest on entering the home was given a very cordial welcome by the relatives of the bride. The hearty reception made every one perfectly at ease. At 3 o'clock Miss Henderson began a pleasing wedding march, the bride and groom entered the beautifully decorated room and took their places beneath an artistically arranged wedding decoration. Rev. Melanchthon Moore, (Brother of our President) performed the sacred rite by which they were legally and scripturally married. The bride looked very pretty and graceful in a dress of brown and the groom in conventional black was at his best. An unusually bountiful repast was tastefully served, adding much to the enjoyment of the guests. The house was very appropriately decorated with vines and flowers. Immediately following the repast President Moore and wife drove to Langdon where they took the train for Reserve, Kansas, the home of the groom's brother. Miss Thompson was one of the very best workers in the Christian Church at Tarkio, a teacher in the public school, and a lady admired by all. All united in wishing that only the best of those things which make life good and worth the living may come to this happy couple, Congratulations are due to Mrs. Thompson and others on being able to make the occasion so agreeable to all. President and Mrs. Moore will be at home in Albany, Mo., after July 10."

The publication went on to describe the reception after the return of Zwinglius and Eda after their honeymoon. "A large number of the friends of President Moore gathered at the chapel last night to welcome him and his bride on their return and to express the kindly regard for them which pervades the entire community. Toasts were offered on "The Bachelor Preacher" and "The Preacher as a Married Man." Prof. Pratt then rendered a guitar solo which was heartily encored, and after a response with another even better than the first, he gave up the platform to Miss Gertrude Edmundson who, in her happy style, delivered a recitation well suited to the occasion. Miss Fanny Stellers followed with a vocal solo which was highly appreciated by all lovers of song. In a few well chosen words Brother D.D. Kingsborough, of the Board of Curators, presented the elegant folding bed and the two stylish and comfortable rockers, the gift of the President's friends, as well as for the beautiful and appropriate presents. Mrs. Moore is a cultured Christian lady and has already won the esteem of our people, who will prove themselves altogether worthy of her friendship."

Eda, also a devoted member of the Disciples of Christ movement, and a public school teacher, supported Zwinglius throughout his career, serving as a hardworking minister's wife in all his congregations. After his death, Eda would continue to work for the Disciples of Christ in a managerial evangelical capacity. Zwinglius and Eda had three children, only one of whom survived -- their son Ferrall, who was born in 1904. Their two daughters died in infancy. Much later, Ferrall wrote a beautiful vignette describing the loving, compassionate relationship that Zwinglius and Eda had.

Zwinglius and Eda left Albany to serve congregations in Paris. Mo., Milford, Ill., Princeton, Mo., Taylorville, Ill., Fort Morgan, Colo., Denning, N. M., and Hoisington, Kansas. They moved to many of these drier corners of the plains states in an effort to find some relief for Zwuinglius's chronically bad asthma, which likely he never found.

Zwinglius came to Hoisington with his family in 1913 and during his residence made a host of friends. He had a kind and noble disposition that won the admiration of all and his death on 23 May 1917 was reported as a "sad shock to the community." During his final years, his son Ferrall wrote, each Sunday's sermon was a greater labor, as Zwinglius's difficulty breathing became more and more desperate. He was widely respected in each of his towns, and his obituary was featured on the front page of the local newspaper, the Hoisington Disptatch. Zwinglius was buried at Tarkio Home Cemetery in Tarkio, Missouri.
