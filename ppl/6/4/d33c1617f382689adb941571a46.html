Narrative

Ferrall Harman Moore was born 27 August 1904 in Taylorville, Illinois, to Erastus Moore and Eda Penick Thompson. As a boy, Ferrall moved with his mother and father, a minister who served First Christian congregations in Missouri, Illinois, Colorado, New Mexico, and Kansas, as his father served different congregations. The family was close and loving, and lived within the humble means of his pastor father. In 1910, at the age of six, Ferrall lived in Deming, New Mexico, a town in the south of New Mexico near Las Cruces. Ferrall probably lived in Fort Morgan, Colorado, for a few years as a very young child, because his father served a congregation there before moving to New Mexico. As they traveled between congregations, his mother, too, dedicated her time to congregational work.

His father died when Ferrall was 12, from the asthma that had led his father to serve congregations in the dry areas of the plain states. Ferrall wrote a beautiful vignette of his memory of his parents' last Christmas together, which portrayed a depth of love and compassion in his family that was truly exceptional. After Ferrall's father died, Ferrall attended Mount Hermon School for Boys in Mount Hermon, Missouri, as his mother's dedication to her missionary-like work caused her to travel often. She died when Ferrall was 21, leaving him without parents or grandparents.

Ferrall started his undergraduate study in West Virginia at Bethany College, a private liberal arts college affiliated with the Christian Church (Disciples of Christ), the denomination for which his father had been a pastor. Both his father and his uncle had attended Bethany. After a year there, he transferred to the University of Missouri for two years, and then finished his undergraduate work at Tarkio College, in Tarkio, Missouri, a traditional liberal arts college with Presbyterian roots, about 100 miles north of Kansas City. His mother lived in Tarkio, where her health begain to fail. He earned his M.A. and Doctor of Medicine degrees at the University of Nebraska Medical School in 1932, and remained a fervent supporter of the University of Nebraska throughout his life, helping to found the alumni organization in the San Francisco Bay Area, and watching the Nebraska-Oklahoma football game each autumn with his family. After graduating from medical school, he moved to San Francisco where he interned a year at U.S. Army Letterman Hospital in the Presidio and served a three-year residency at St. Luke's Hospital. He took the California medical exam in 1933, and passed with the highest score of his exam class.

Ferrall worked as a physician throughout his professional life. After finishing his residency at St. Luke's Hospital, he was offered the position of ship's doctor on one of the Matson Line's passenger ships, the "Monterey" or "Mariposa," that travelled the Pacific Ocean. This posting was traditionally offered to the senior resident at St. Luke's. On one trip his ship berthed at Suva, Fiji for the day. While there Ferrall called in at the local hospital to meet some of the doctors. During that day he was invited to tea with some of the nurses and doctors. A nurse from New Zealand, Annie Edith Beatrice "Bee" Telford, was a guest at tea also as she was visiting an acquaintance of a nurse friend in New Zealand. Ferrall learned she was a passenger on his ship, buy didn't let on at the time that he wasn't a local doctor. That evening, he turned up in his tropical white ship's uniform at the head of her dining table! They danced after dinner and he started wooing Bee in a whirlwind romance! Ferrall proposed either the second or third day after they met. Bee was on her way to Canada to visit her half-brother Jack in Toronto and then go on to England to do some advanced nursing. When the ship called into San Francisco, Ferrall showed Bee all over the city. Ferrall wanted Bee to change her plans and have her change her plans to meet him in Minnesota where he was to be a fellow at the Mayo Clinic. Bee went on to Canada to her brother's home and just before Christmas learned that her mother had died suddenly and had made her husband promise not to tell Bee prior to Christmas. After learning this, Bee booked a passage back to New Zealand to make sure her father was settled down, had a housekeeper, and would live comfortably in her absense. She later tried to book a ship from Auckland directly to England, but had to take a ship to Sydney and then transfer to another ship. You guessed it, it was a Matson Line ship to Sydney with Ferrall aboard. Ferrall bought Bee's engagement and wedding band for her in Sydney prior to waiving her off to England. Bee stayed approximately three months there before embarking on a new life in the U.S. beginning with her wedding to Ferrall on September 8, 1936. He was a fellow in medicine at the Mayo Clinic from 1936-1938. Their first child, Beatrice ("BeBe") Eda was born in Rochester, Minnesota in 1937. Their second child, Michael Telford, was born in San Francisco in 1939; and, finally their third child, Pamela Helen, was born in Palo Alto in 1941.

During World War Two, Ferrall enlisted in the U.S. Navy, and was assigned as a Lieutenant Commander to the 1st Marine Air Wing which evacuated Marines from Henderson Field on Guadalcanal. He witnessed some of the most horrific action of the war in the Pacific theater, while commanding the air evacuation of casualties. He engineered the system which evacuated over 2,000 casualties without suffering a single death. For his service, he was awarded the Legion of Merit, a military decoration of the United States armed forces awarded for exceptionally meritorious conduct in the performance of outstanding services and achievements. The Legion of Merit is one of only two United States decorations to be issued as a neck order (the other being the Medal of Honor), and the only United States decoration which may be issued in award degrees.

Immediately following the war, he helped found the Redwood City Clinic in Redwood City, where he was an internist for 20 years before going into institutional medicine in 1965. He was the first Chief of Staff at Sequoia Hospital in Redwood City; at the dedication ceremony for the hospital, Earl Warren, who was then the Governor of California and later would become the Chief Justice of the U.S. Supreme Court, dedicated the hospital and spoke to the crowd.

Ferrall and Bee remained in Redwood City while their children, BeBe, Mike, and Pam, finished their schooling. In 1951 he was elected to the Sequoia High School District Board of Trustees, and served for three years. He also served as president of the San Mateo County medical Society from 1957-1958.

In 1969, Ferrall accepted a post as physician specialist at Mendocino State Hospital at Ukiah, about a two-hour's drive north of the San Francisco peninsula. He and Bee stayed in Ukiah for the next four years. In 1973, they returned to the Peninsula where Ferrall worked at the Crystal Springs Rehabilitation Center in Belmont and the Hassler Tuberculosis Sanitarium Hospital in Redwood City, staying a couple of years before returning to Ukiah to work for the Professional Standards Review Board. He retired in 1979 and he and Bee moved to Cloverdale.

An internal medicine specialist, he was a member of the California Academy of Medicine and a Fellow of the American College of Physicians. He was a member of Alpha Omega Alpha Medical Scholastic Honor Society, a society for the most academically accomplished medical students chosen by their medical school faculty. He also belonged to the Nu Sigma Nu Medical Fraternity, and was an alumnus of the prestigious Mayo Clinic Foundation. When he took his California Medical Exams for state licensure he placed second in the entire state. Despite these recognitions, he remained humble and selfless.

Ferrall had several interests, including woodworking and chess. He enjoyed working with several large-scale power woodworking tools in his garage, and made items like a monogrammed toy chest for his grandchildren. He also made Bee a cedar chest, desk, curio display table and Pam a butler's tray and end table. He made spoon racks for all the women in the family also. He played chess often with his grandson, Richard, and belonged to local chess clubs, especially in Ukiah. He was a devoted husband, father and grandfather. His grandson, Richard, remembered that when Ferrall was in his 70s, he secretly wrote "Ferrall loves Bee" on a whiteboard on their refrigerator. Richard also remembers Bee's love for Ferrall, as she playfully chastised him for sneaking some of her legendary cookies from the kitchen late at night. He was a consistent member of church communities wherever he lived. He was a member of Masonic Abell Lodge 146 and the Pomo Shrine Club, an affiliate of Aahmes Temple. No doubt his humble father and mother would have been proud of how his life epitomized the service to others that they modeled.
