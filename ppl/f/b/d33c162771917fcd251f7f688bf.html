Narrative

Emma Long was born in London about 1806. About 1830 she married John Cooke, a 29-year-old calico printer who would come to own his own textile mill in the thriving Manchester textile trade. Their adulthood in Manchester would have been an interesting time, as Manchester was the cite of frequent working-class uprisings demanding better working conditions and equitable representation in Parliament. Emma, as the wife of a mill owner, would have been familiar with work stoppages, but may also have been sympathetic to the extension of political rights to the middle class.

Emma and John had six children between 1831 and 1840. They gave each child the same middle name, Long, which suggests either that their family followed a custom of using the mother's maiden name as the middle name, or that the Long family name was sufficiently well known that that they hoped to preserve their children's association with it. In fact, their children typically used their middle name on official documents in places where many people did not, which suggests that Emma Long's family may have been influential. For example, Emma's eldest son was referred to as "Richard Long Cooke" on his military enrollment, military resignation, and marriage certificates. Even Richard's wife's death certificate referred to her as "wife of Richard Long Cooke." Other than this emphasis of her maiden name, we don't know anything about Emma's parents or childhood. The first record we have located for her is the 1840 birth certificate of her youngest child, Adeline Long Cooke.

Emma lived her adult life near Manchester, a town that was dominated during Victorian times by the unbridled production of cotton textiles. Emma's husband, John, owned a textile mill there, which was run by their son Richard after John's death. John died before 1861, likely in 1852, probably with his health compromised by the soot and lint pollution for which the city was infamous.

Emma died in 1865 in Blackpool, on the coast just west of Manchester, of chronic bronchitis which she had suffered for many years. Her son, Richard, suffered from severe asthma during his adulthood, so it is possible that Emma too suffered from asthma. In the heavily polluted atmosphere of Manchester, most deaths were attributed to breathing difficulties, such as tuberculosis, asthma, and bronchitis. After Emma's death, her and John's property was passed to Richard through an official probate action, which suggests that they were of significant middle-class means to justify such a proceeding.
