Narrative

John Coe was born June 15, 1649, in colonial New England, in Gloucester, Massachusetts, only 19 years after the founding of the Massachusetts Bay Colony. He was the son of Matthew and Elizabeth (Wakley) Coe. Matthew had immigrated from England to Portsmouth, New Hampshire, in 1640. Although many of the early New England colonists were not religious, the great majority of them were Calvinist Protestants who fled oppression in England. It is reasonable to suspect that John's father was a Calvinist and had left England for this reason. 1640, the year when Matthew left England, was a turbulent time, during the uncertainty and bitter fueding between Parliament and the king which would lead to the outbreak of the English Civil War in 1641. It is possible that this atmosphere of conflict helped to convince Matthew of the need to emmigrate. Also, by 1640, the Massachusetts Bay Colony had proved to be successful, and the difficult years of the colony's founding were over, so the option of moving to Massachusetts was likely more attractive than it had been, say, in 1630.

John went north with his parents in 1661 to Falmouth, in what would become the state of Maine. In 1676, King Philip's War broke out, in which the New England colonists fought a bitter war against Native American tribes who resisted the seizure of their land. Falmough was was destroyed by Indians, and his family fled to Duxbury, in Plymouth Colony, nearby where the "pilgrim" Separatists had founded the first of the Massachusetts colonies.

John woked as a surveyor and settler of boundaries for the Plymouth Colony. In this capacity, he came down the Seconnet River to Little Compton, Rhode Island, about the time the town was settled. Upon arriving there he went to the home of William Pabodie, where he remained "until his work was done." While there, he must have fallen in love with Sarah Pabodie, William's daughter, because he married her on Nov. 10, 1681, at Duxbury. They returned to Little Compton, where they lived for over 40 years, probably earning their living through farming.

When John died on December 16, 1728, his estate was inventoried. It contained "wearing apparel sea chests and instruments. 8 cows 2 oxen, 70 acres of land with houses" worth a total of 1400.00 pounds.
