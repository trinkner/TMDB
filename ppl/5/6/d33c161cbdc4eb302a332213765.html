Narrative

Henry Tompkins seems to have dedicated his life to combat the problems that most affected his youth. Although his first born child died tragically at about the age of three, Henry served as a lifelong leader of the YMCA movement to provide opportunities for children. Although he never finished high school himself, he served on the board of directors of a college and taught Sunday school. Although he rose from the uneducated working class to corporate management, he strove to provide fair salaries to workers.

Henry Tompkins was a self-made railroad executive and devoted father and husband who was well loved and respected by his community. He road the coattails of railroad development in the Midwest to financial success and professional prestige despite never finishing high school. The humble nature of his origins and the hard work his childhood required of him led him to value both education and the common man, and to dedicate his life to providing wholesome growth opportunities to others, and to ensuring that the common working man got a fair shake.

He was born 3 May 1864, a year before the end of the Civil War, in Galesburg, Illinois, the town his grandfather, Samuel Tompkins, had helped to found. He was the third of four children, and was born when his father, Franklin, was rather old, at 44. Franklin had worked as a teacher, and was working as a carpenter when Henry was born. His mother was Samantha Ward, who was born in Pennsylvania but grew up in Berwick, Illinois.

Henry's childhood family was probably of humble means, as his father worked in two low-paying careers, working as a teacher and later as a carpenter. Nevertheless, the family did own their own home and held a mortgage, so they probably lived in lower-middle-class means. When Henry was just a young teen, he left the public schools to support the family, because his father was either ill or injured. At the time, both his older brother and older sister were also working to support the family, William as a depot caller and Cora as a dressmaker. Henry's first job was as a Western Union telegram delivery boy, and he shortly worked his way up to working as a telegraph operator and a bookkeeper.

He began railroad work in 1877 as a messenger boy on the Chicago, Burlington & Quincy railroad, and remained on that road until 1904, serving successively as clerk, telegraph operator, car distributor, train dispatcher, chief dispatcher and trainmaster.

By 1884, Katherine Leach, also of Galesburg, had caught Henry's eye. She was nearly three years younger than he was, and the two fell in love, perhaps without the approval of Henry's father, Franklin. Katherine became pregnant, and she and Henry hoped to marry, but they were under age and could not marry without their parents' consent. Franklin refused to give his consent. By April 1885, Katherine was nine months pregnant, and still Franklin refused. Then, on April 22, three momentous events took place on the same day for Henry: his father died, Henry got married, and Katherine gave birth to their first child. Henry's mother, Samantha consented to the marriage and signed the marriage certificate.

Henry and Katherine had four children, Albert, Katherine, Ruth, and Paul, all born in Galesburg. Sadly, Albert, born the day of his parents' marriage, died as a toddler. He was walking down stairs when a girl going more quickly behind him bumped him, and he fell.

In the early 1900s, the Chicago, Burlington & Quincy railroad sent him briefly to Nebraska to install a block signal system, probably on the line to Omaha which the company had opened in 1899. After this trip, he considered moving to Nebraska. After this installation, the Lake Shore and Michigan Southern Railway asked the Illinois Central to lend him for a year to install block signal systems for them on their lines. Henry and the companies agreed, and so and his family he moved to Cleveland. There, he also revised the company's book of rules. Early in 1905 he was appointed Assistant Superintendent at Buffalo. He transferred permanently to the Lake Shore railroad, which was purchased by Vanderbilt as he expanded the New York Central railroad. Thereafter, Henry and his family lived in cities served the New York Central's famed "water route." After living a year in Cleveland, in about 1909 he transferred to Buffalo, another city on the water route.

In Buffalo, he served as division superintendent for the railroad. One episode there which affected him greatly was a large flood in nearby Erie, which was featured in many newspaper articles. As a railroad superintendent, Henry waded out in the cold flood waters, and experienced his first heart trouble as a result. He would have a weak heart for the rest of his life.

By 1912, he had risen to superintendent of the Michigan Central Railroad, another railroad operated by the New York Central. He was mentioned in a New York Times about the tendency of train engineers to ignore signal lights. Henry participated in an inspection committee to determine why engineers ignored the signals, and how best to encourage them to pay better attention in order to avoid needless accidents.

By 1920, he had been transferred back to Cleveland where he worked as the head of the railroad's wage bureau, which required him to negotiate contracts between the company and the various labor unions. Through this experience, he became recognized as a reliable expert in labor negotiations with the unions, and was respected as a fair man by both management and the union leaders. Perhaps because of his humble origins and lack of formal education, he could relate well to the working man, while still respecting the obligations and responsibilities of the management whose ranks he had worked to join. His granddaughter, Helen, remembered that as a child when she would go into his office to collect him at the end of the work day, his colleagues adored him.

Once the children had grown, Henry gave each of them the choice of attending college, taking private music lessons at home or working. Katherine chose music lessons, and studied piano. Ruth chose to work. Paul chose to attend Case University, and studied chemistry.

Unfortunately, in 1927, Katherine suffered a severe stroke while visiting him in New York City while he was there on business. Henry returned to their hotel room only to find it locked. Unable to open the door, he and others had to break down the door. Inside the room they found Katherine, collapsed. Because of Henry's position at the railroad, he was given a private rail car to transport her back to Cleveland. The railroad workers had to remove a window from the car for Katherine to be inserted into the car on a stretcher. A doctor and nurse traveled with them in the car from New York to Cleveland. When they arrived in Cleveland, Henry's son-in-law, Ralph Trinkner, who also worked for the railroad, had arranged for railroad men to remove the window and help remove Katherine from the car. She survived for 12 more years, but never regained her full capacities. Slowly, she learned to speak a few phrases and could sit up in bed. After time, she was able to walk around the house, so long as she had a railing or furniture to steady herself. Henry was devoted to her, and installed railings throughout the house for her.

Henry was a life-long devoted member of the Baptist church, and adhered closely to the principles of the church. He did not drink alcohol, and was a regular participant in the church community. He served as chairman of church's board of deacons and as the church moderator, and taught Sunday school. He was president of the Cleveland Baptist association. One story, however, shows that he was not dogmatically inflexible. Once, while working in Buffalo he was responsible for leading an inspection tour of the railroad line, accompanied by visiting railroad executives. They took a private train to inspect the line in western New York, and visited a winery served by the line. The owners of the winery gave Henry and the others a glass of wine during their visit, which Henry drank. Upon hearing the story, his daughter was incensed that he had drunk the wine. He explained that because he was in charge of the inspection tour, he had been obligated to drink the wine so that everybody else would drink it, enjoy the tour, and not offend the client. Evidently, his daughter saw the issue in a different light after he explained it to her!

Henry was also a fervent supporter of the Young Men's Christian Association (YMCA). He served as the president of the Cleveland YMCA, and was a member of the board of managers of the YMCA-supported Fenn College in Cleveland (which in the 1960s would become Cleveland State University). Throughout his career, and especially at functions related to the college, he was often asked which college he attended.

Henry was a 1st Lieutenant in the Illinois militia, which became Company C, of the Sixth Infantry of the Illinois National Guard. This company saw duty in helping to maintain the peace during labor strikes, although we don't know whether Henry served during these conflicts.

He loved to go out to dinner, and frequently told his daughter, "You drive and we'll go out to dinner."

Henry died at home of heart failure in Jan 1933, when he was 67. Three years earlier, according to the 1930 census, he was still working for the railroad.
