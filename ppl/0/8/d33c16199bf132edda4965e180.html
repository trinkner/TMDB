Narrative

Michael Moore's life was one of a childhood in an exciting and dangerous time in a new state on the western frontier of a growing country. Sadly his life ended when he was only 32 in tragic circumstances.

Michael Moore was born in 1809 in Captina Creek, a little-settled farming area on the banks of the Ohio river. Only six years earlier President Jefferson had signed the bill that admitted Ohio into the union. Napoleon ruled over most of western Europe, and had just sold to the United States the vast land holdings from the Mississippi to the Rocky Mountains. Michael grew up on his father's farm in this corner of Belmont County, which has been settled in part by his grandfather, also named Michael Moore, whom Michael would have known well. At the time, the county experienced violent conflicts between the Anglo settlers and the native Shawnee tribe. As a boy, Michael probably played in the woods around his father's farm, but he would have been very conscious of the danger posed to settlers by the Shawnee.

When Michael was three years old, his father, Jacob Moore, left the farm to fight in the War of 1812. Ohio had been on the front lines of the conflicts with Native Americans, as Tecumseh, the leader of the Shawnee, had lived in Ohio and had fought against the Anglo settlers as they spread westward. Tecumseh's father had been killed in the 1770s not far from Belmont County. Jacob returned from the war as lieutenant, and probably enjoyed strong respect from the county's families.

On 20 August 1832, when he was 23, Michael married Priscilla Deaver of Maryland. They lived in Belmont county, where Michael worked as a cabinet maker. They had four children, including Erastus Moore, who was born in 1833.

Sadly, just two weeks after their last child, Michael Emmer, was born, Michael died at the age of 32 in Baltimore. He died of a morphine overdose, the circumstances of which we don't currently know. At the time in the United States, morphine use was expanding rapidly, both as a commonly prescribed medication for dysentery and cholera, as well as an abused narcotic. By the 1860s, morphine addiction was a significant problem, especially in larger east-coast cities. We also don't know why he died in Baltimore. His wife's family was from Maryland, so perhaps he was visiting relatives. As a cabinet maker, perhaps he was in Baltimore to buy supplies for his business; Baltimore would have been a nearby seaport for Belmont County, Ohio, although Cleveland and the Great Lakes would have been closer.

His wife, Priscilla, later married Eli Mantle, with whom she had three more children who grew up as close siblings with the children she had had with Michael. Michael's father, Jacob, left considerable property in Belmont county to Michael's four children in his will.
