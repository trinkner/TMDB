Telford, Thomas Fletcher

Narrative

Tom Telford lived his life across the globe and took full advantage of the opportunities that colonialism offered a young, enterprising man. Born from parents in humble circumstances in Louisiana, raised in Canada and Ireland, and left to live more or less on his own at a young age, he rose to become one of the most respected medical doctors in New Zealand.

Thomas Fletcher Telford, who always went by "Tom" but signed letters "T. Fletcher Telford," was born of an Irish immigrant, John Gardiner Telford, and his wife, Eliza Jane Blue, of Texas. He was born in or near Monroe, Louisiana, on 27 May 1876. The middle name "Fletcher" likely came from the Fletcher family that was closely connected to the Telfords in Ireland. The uncle-in-law of John, Thomas's father, was Wellesley Pole Fletcher, a well known Irish lawyer. (Wellesley Pole Fletcher was the husband of Sally Elizabeth Telford, the sister of John Telford.)

Thomas's father had fought for the Union in the Ameircan Civil War, immediately after emigrating to the US from King's Count, Ireland. Perhaps his duties in the war or after the war led him to live in Louisiana. Shortly after Thomas's birth, his parents moved to Toronto, Canada, where he lived for his early boyhood. He appeared on the 1881 Canadian census, and was baptized in Toronto in 1877. His mother left the family around 1888 to return to the South where, according to family memory, she entered a sanitarium and died in Arkansas, apparently due to a desire to be near family friends, the Wynns, a family that appears living in the house next door to Eliza Jane's house in the 1870 census before she married Thomas's father.

After his mother's death, Thomas and his sisters Annie and Alice were sent to Ireland to be raised by their maiden aunt, Anna Telford, and perhaps their grandmother, Jane Maria (Fletcher) Telford, who died in Dublin in 1896. Thomas's father continued to live as an employed farm hand or odd job worker in Lewiston, Niagara, New York, and ultimately died in Liverpool, England, having never returned to live in his native Ireland. Thomas's aunt, Anna Telford lived at 26 Lansdowne Road, Dublin, which is the address that Thomas would also use while studying medicine in Dublin. Thomas attended Wellington School, a private boy's school in Shropshire, England, where he earned several academic awards. He then attended the Trinity University in Dublin, where he earned a BA, taking a "third-class" BA in 1898, and further earning BCh, and BAO degrees. While studying at Trinity, he studied a variety of medical specialities in Dublin hospitals. He was associated for at least three years with the Sir Patrick Dun's Hospital, serving as a clinical clerk and finally as a resident. During this period he also studied at the Rotunda Hospital. In these residency studies, he studied obstetrics (delivering 16 babies), general surgery and clinical practice. He earned a Bachelor's degree in medicine on 23 February 1901. After earning this degree, he remained in Dublin, studying clinical opthalmology until January 1902.

In early 1902 he joined the British war effort as a civil surgeon for the final months of the Second Boer War, which was fought between 1898 and 1902. He was attached to the Royal Army Medical Corps. On the ship sailing to South Africa an outbreak of typhoid fever resulted in many deaths among the soldiers, which aroused in Thomas an interest in communicable disease and preventive medicine which was later to be of great value and importance in New Zealand. He returned to Ireland when the war ended, sailing on the Staffordshire, which departed the colony on 22 July 1902 and arrived in London on 4 August 1902.

He returned to Dublin to complete his medical studies at Trinity College. He also attended lectures at Fever Hospital and studied mental diseas as Richmond District Asylum. He gained his Doctorate degree in medicine 20 December 1902, and registered the passage of his medical board examinations on 17 June 1903.

In October 1903, he returned to the Cape of Good Hope Colony to establish a civilian medical practice. He was granted a licence to practice medicine there on 15 October 1903. Evidently, he established his practice in Darling, on the western coast of the Colony. In November 1903, he paid five pounds to license a practice in Darling. He practiced medicine in the colony for two years, until 1905; according to a letter of introduction that he later carried to New Zealand, he left the colony because he "didn't like the country." Family notes also suggest that he returned to Ireland because his aunt asked him to come home.

Thomas's stay in Ireland, however, was brief. According to family memory, he decided to go to the British Colony farthest away from Ireland and so decided to establish a medical practice in New Zealand. A letter of introduction indicated that he had become engaged to a woman named Janet with a surname that begins with "S." (I cannot decipher the handwriting of the letter to know her full surnamel.) However, when he sailed for New Zealand, he sailed alone, with no women of her name on the passenger list. He departed for Wellington from London aboard the S.S. Turakina of the New Zealand Shipping Company, departing from London on 14 July 1905.

Once he arrived in Wellington, he applied for the superintendent of the hospital in the small mining south-island town of Ross, on the southern island. His application was approved, and on 25 September 1905 he sailed aboard the Wainui to Ross -- an event that was was worthy of metion in the local newspaper. He was accepted as a Fellow of the Royal Institute of Public Health on 6 October 1905. In Ross, he met Mary Edith "Edith" Cooke who worked as a postmistress of the town. She had been married once, but her first husband had died. She had a son by her first marriage who was 17 years older than her daughter by her second marriage. This son, Jack Langdon, later emigrated to Canada. Tom and Mary Edith had one child, a daughter, Annie Edith Beatrice Telford, who always went by "Bee." Bee later moved to the United States and lived much of her life in northern California.

During World War 1, Thomas was appointed Captain in the New Zealand land forces as a doctor. He was kept in New Zealand during the war because his medical position was deemed sufficiently important to the civilian community. Thomas was later in general practice until he was appointed superintendent of the nearby Greymouth Hospital. Owing to his qualifications and interest in public health, he was offered the position of medical officer in the New Northland district. After a short time there, he was appointed medical officer of the Canterbury district in 1920. He served this district continuously until his retirement in 1945.

Tom's first wife, Edith, died in 1935. He married again, to Alice Gwendoline "Gwen" Philipps; they remained married thoughout his life.

He was the driving force behind the public health campaign which saw the mass immunization of children against diphtheria and the removal of this disease from the killing-disease list. During this time his interest in preventive medicine served the community well, because there were several outbreaks of typhoid fever and Dr Telford with determination and tireless devotion never failed to locate and isolate the sources of these outbreaks. Moreover, by his efforts in constantly improving sanitation and other public health measures, he lessened the causes of these epidemics. Other epidemic diseases, such as diphtheria also gained his attention, resulting in constant improvements in this and other diseases. He contributed to research on the spread of rickets, by surveying dozens of doctors in the Wellington area. He can be regarded as one of New Zealand's pioneers in the field of preventive medicine. In the mid-1940s, he served as Chairman of the Leper's Trust Board, and oversaw the donation by the board of over 15,000 pounds to improve the lives of New Zealand lepers on the island of Makogai.

Telford enjoyed good health in retirement. He was often seen dressed in a neat suit, carrying his trademark bag, and walking briskly through his Christchurch home suburb, St. Martins. He lived at 59 Aynsley Terrace. On his 90th birthday, he said that his eyesight was 'practically normal' and gave, as his recipe for long life, 'regular exercise, a good constitution, not being too good and not being too bad'. He continued to live in Christchurch and enjoyed his hobby of gardening. He was a foundation member of the lily Society of New Zealand and a member diphtheria the Canterbury Horticultural Society. He was one of the last surviving members of the South African War Veterans' Association in New Zealand. He also belonged to the Royal Society of New Zealand, an organization designed to foster in the New Zealand community a culture that supports science and technology.

He was a sincere and deeply religious man, a member of St Mark's Church, Opawa, and had been a life-long Mason. According to his obituary, he was a wealth of anecdotes, graphically told and acted. H had a never-failing sense of humor and had an inherent grace of manner.

Tom did not realize that his Louisiana birth had made him an American citizen until he went to join the New Zealand Public Health Service. It was at that time that he actually became British!
