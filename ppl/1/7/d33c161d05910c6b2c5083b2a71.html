Ward, Samantha A.

Birth Name Ward, Samantha A.

Narrative

In the 1830s, western Illinois was unsettled prairie, a dream of unclaimed farmland awaiting the families of the eastern U.S. hungry for new land and the prosperity that cheap land promised. Samantha Ward grew up the daughter of a farmer who seized this opportunity, and then married the son of a man who had helped found Galesburg, one of the leading experimental communities on the prairie. By the time she died, she had seen the breaking of the prairies and the growth of western Illinois into a modern network of rural towns, linked by the new technologies of the railroad, telephone, and automobiles.

Samantha Ward was born to a farming family in Pennsylvania, probably in Rutland of Tioga county. Between 1830 and 1840, when Samantha was just a small girl, her father moved the family westward to the newly opened farmland of western Illinois. The family helped to "break the prairies" in this unsettled portion of Illinois, just 30 miles from the Iowa border. The nearby town of Galesburg, founded in 1837, would have been the nearest town of any size. As the family lived on a farm, Samantha probably spent her childhood helping her parents around the property, probably helping her mother in the kitchen around an iron wood stove and with raising the smaller animals on the farm, like chickens and geese. It's likely that she knew how to ride a horse.

On 24 June 1851, when she was 20, Samantha married Franklin Tompkins, who was ten years older than she. They married in Berwick, the new town close to her family's farm, and then moved to Galesburg, the town that Franklin's father, Samuel, had helped to found 14 years earlier. They would have been a well known family, as in Galesburg there was a central street named Tompkins, named after Samuel.

Samantha was also likely religious, as her father was a Baptist preacher in addition to being a farmer. Galesburg had been founded as an experimental community dedicated to Protestant values, and became the national epicenter for the abolition movement and the Underground Railroad. Franklin was likely raised in a devout family, as his father had helped found Galesburg in part for religious motivations.

Samantha and Franklin were likely of humble but adequate means, as he worked as a carpenter in this growing town. He likely rarely lacked for work, as the town's population grew and continually needed new housing. Between 1859 and 1871 they had four children, and Samantha worked as a homemaker to raise the children.

Apparently, the Civil War little affected Samantha or her immediate family. When the war broke out in 1861, her husband was 39 and too old to consider enlisting as many younger men did. As a father of young children, he also needed to stay home to provide for the family. Their sons were too young to join the service, so none of their sons fought in the war.

By 1880, Franklin was working as a "Master Carpenter," perhaps employing other carpenters, and so their financial situation was likely better than adequate. Franklin's health, however, probably began to suffer, as family memory holds that he was ill towards the end of his life and perhaps not able to work full time. In 1880, when Samantha was 50, their children were working to help support the family. William was 21 and working as a "depot caller." Cora, 18, worked as a dressmaker. Henry was 16 and working as a bookkeeper after having worked for a year or two as a Western Union telegram boy. Only Mary, 9, was still in school.

Just before her husband died, the family seemed to have been torn by disagreement. Her 19-year-old son Henry had fallen in love with a young Katherine Leach, who was pregnant with Henry's child. Both Henry and Katherine were under 21, and so needed their parents' permission to marry. For reasons we don't currently know, Franklin refused to give his consent, despite the lengthening of Katherine's pregnancy and her approaching due date. Franklin died on 22 April 1885 in the ninth month of Katherine's pregnancy. On that same day Henry's mother gave her consent for the marriage to go forward, and Henry and Katherine wed. That same day Katherine gave birth to a son. At the time of this drama, Samantha was 55.

In 1897, Samantha suffered the blow of watching her youngest daughter May Ella die of consumption. May Ella had been sick with consumption, probably tuberculosis, for several years, and her symptoms had come and gone over the years.

By 1900, Samantha had moved to live with her daughter, Cora, and her husband Fremont Ballou. She died on 29 May 1905 in Galesburg, having lived in the area for about 70 years. During her lifetime, she saw the creation of the prairie frontier towns, the coming of the railroad, the electrification of Galesburg, the growth of the telephone network, and even the first automobiles. Her son Henry would go on to ride the growth of these new industries, becoming a successful railroad executive as Galesburg became an important stop on two of the century's new railroads, the Chicago, Burlington & Quincy Railroad, and later, the Santa Fe Railroad.
