Narrative

Richard Long Cooke lived a life that paralleled Victorian England. He was born six years before Queen Victoria assumed the English throne, and died five years after Victoria died. He knew first-hand the prosperity of unregulated English industry, yet also suffered tremendously from the pollution that this unbridled production created. He joined the British army during the 1860s, and later experienced the growth of a young English colony in New Zealand, where he died in the clean air some 20 years after escaping the squalor of Manchester that had claimed his wife when she was only 43.

Richard was born on 14 April 1830 to John and Emma Cooke, in Manchester, England, and christened on 14 May 1830 at the Collegiate Church (later renamed the Manchester Cathedral) in Manchester. His father was a profitable calico salesman in the textile business near Manchester, a region famous at the time for its textile industry. In the 1840s, steam power was a new invention to speed the manufacture of cloth, and Manchester was the center of the English textile trade. Unfortunately, this industry polluted the air terribly in Manchester, and the coal soot and lint dust led to widespread asthma and other resperatory ailments. Richard's mother was born Emma Long. The family was likely proud of its connection to the Long family, because each of Richard's siblings was given the Long name as their middle name, and they often went by their full name, such as on census reports, when most people only went by their first and surnames.

In 1862, Richard joined the 1st Manchester or 6th Lancashire Rifle Volunteer corps. On 27 March 1862, he was given the rank of Ensign. He was promoted to Captain in 1863, although he resigned from the service that same year. the Lancashire Rifle Volunteer corps was a type of militia, like the US Army Reserves, that primarily served locally but could be called up to serve in foreign wars. As the unit names suggests, members were volunteers. The government provided uniforms, but the members were not paid. In the early 1860s, many Manchester men joined the LRV due to fear of an invasion by France, which has recently fought against Austria. In the 1860s, these volunteers were not used internationally, and membership seems to have been mostly a badge of patriotic duty.

Bee Moore, Richard's granddaughter, remembered that Richard may have inherited his father's cotton mill in Manchester, England. We don't know when his father died, but it was probably after 1851. The Ancestry.com entry for the 1851 census listed Richard's sister, Adeline, as having parents John and Emma, which implies that John was still alive in 1851. Richard probably would not have enlisted in the military if his father were dead, because Richard would then have run the family business. It is possible that he resigned from the service in 1863 because of the death of his father. We know with certainty that both his parents had died by 1865, because in that year when he rved as the executor of the estate of his his mother, the probate documents refered to her as a widow.

Richard married Mary Jane Bridge in 1865. The couple had five children: Mary Edith, George Frederick, Gertrude, Frances, and Henry Philip. Richard worked in the same business as his father, both as a "calico printer" and as a cloth "salesman." Family memory holds that he traveled often, perhaps to Asia to acquire cotton or to sell his fabric.

Accounts of Manchester of this time recount horrifying air quality and resulting sickness, which affected both Richard and his wife. Coal-burning domestic fires and innumerable factory chimneys meant that the city was overhung with a permanent pall of smoke, drenched with acid rain, and suffered plagues of respiratory diseases. The cotton lint and dust which hung in the air dramatically shortened life expectancy. "Mill Fever", aching head, limbs and nausea were common. Both Richard and Mary Jane suffered from such afflictions; Richard escaped with his life to New Zealand, but Mary Jane was to die in 1882 at the age of 43.

In 1878, Mary Jane had a photograph taken of her and the children, as well as a portrait taken of her. She looks in the photograph to be of robust health. However, shortly after this photo was taken, she began to suffer from some sort of cerebral disease, perhaps the mill fever described above, and moved to live in the Beech House hospital facility in Stockport, leaving Richard to live with his son Frederick and a servant, and for the other children to live as boarders in a nearby house with a governess. This living arrangement, and the fact that Richard could afford a servant and a governess, suggests that he had prospered as a cloth merchant. It also lends credence to family memory that he had inherited his father's factory. It is also true that servants were more affordable in the 1880s than today.

Richard apparently remained in England until Mary Jane died, in Septeber 1882. He then moved to New Zealand with his family to escape the foul air of Manchester. Bee Moore noted that Richard was an asthmatic, a condition commonly caused then by the exposure to lint while working in fabric mills. In 1882, he was about 50, and so was young enough to live many more years of life in cleaner air. Richard moved the family to New Zealand in 1883.

Richard lived his old age with his daughter Mary Edith, who became the postmistress of Ross, New Zealand in 1902. In his last years he suffered increasingly from asthma and chest pains. He died in Ross on in mid-summer, on 20 January 1904, at the age of 73, reportedly from the effects of harshly hot weather in Ross. He is buried in the church graveyard in Ross. His obituary describes him as a man of gentlemanly bearing who was on friendly terms with all.
