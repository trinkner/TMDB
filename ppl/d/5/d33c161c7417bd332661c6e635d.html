Narrative

Katheryn Berger was born in Cleveland in 1875, and lived the rest of her life in Cleveland. She was the daughter of German and Dutch immigrants who had arrived in the United States in the middle of the 1800s. Her father, Frederick W. Berger, came from Berlin in 1862, and her mother, Berendina Dent, came from the Netherlands in 1856. We don't know which language she spoke with her parents in their home.

Kathryn's childhood was likely a difficult one, as her parents were of humble means. It's possible that Kathryn lived for some time on a dair farm, as family memory holds that her parents operated a dairy farm in the 1880s until a tramp accidentally started a fire that burned down their barn. By 1900, six of Kathryn's ten siblings had died, including one boy who died after falling into a pail of scalding water, and one infant girl who died as a result of the fire.

Katheryn married Paul W. Trinkner in Cleveland in 1895 when she was twenty years old. Paul also was the son of two recently arrived Germanic immigrant families. His father, Paul F. Trinkner, came to Cleveland from Germany in 1865, while his mother, Mary (nee Kappus) also came from Germany as a young woman.

Kathryn's adult life seems to have been much happier than that of her mother, although sadly she died quite young. She worked as a housewife and a mother throughout her life, while her husband worked as a "pattern maker" in a steel works, which was a better-than-average skilled industrial job making the molds for industrially produced tools and goods. They owned a nice middle-class house in Cleveland. In 1900, they lived on Bower Street, next door to Kathryn's sister and her husband, Amelia and John Graul. They had three children: a son Ralph, and identical twin daughters Elsie and Edna. Ralph went to work at a very young age, after dropping out of school at age 13, evidently to help support the family. Elsie and Edna led highly musical lives, and were famous in Cleveland for their ability to play classical piano.

Kathryn died in Cleveland on 8 May 1925 when she was only 49. After having surgery for an ulcer, her appendix burst, and she died of peritonitis.
