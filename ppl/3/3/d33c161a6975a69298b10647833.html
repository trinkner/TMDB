Narrative

Captain William Neville was an early, but brief resident of Rutherford county, North Carolina. His parentage is unknown, though it is supposed by many that he was a son of Joseph Neville and Ann Bohannan, but proof of this relationship is still lacking. The first record known for William is in 1760 when he "farm lets" 200 acres of land in the Manor of Leeds, Fauquier county, Virginia. The rental agreement is for the duration of his natural life and also for that of his wife, Winefred, and son, Jesse. A similar document for 202 acres in the Manor of Leeds was made in 1763 listing his wife and son, Yelverton. By 1768 he had moved to North Carolina as he witnessed a deed in Tryon County at that time.

William served in two wars. He is shown as an ensign in the Fauquier county, Virginia, militia in 1762 during the the French and Indian War. He was also a captain in the Revolutionary War stationed on the frontiers of Rutherford and Tryon counties, North Carolina. William never received a pension but references to his service can be found in a pay voucher at the NC State Archives and also in the pension records of his son, Yelverton . His son, Jesse, also served in the Revolutionary War and was pensioned.

For many years William served as a Justice of the Peace. The first record of this position is found in Tryon county, North Carolina in 1776. He continued as a Justice after the new county of Rutherford was formed, through 1789. He also served as a sheriff in Rutherford county.

By 1790, William was a resident of Pendleton county, South Carolina, with three males under 16, seven females and three slaves. From 1792-1794 he was a Justice of the Peace. By 1799 he had moved again, this time to Barren county, Kentucky, where he lived through 1808. In 1808 he gave a power of attorney to sell his Barren county land and about this time probably moved to White county, Tennessee.

William married Wineford Oldham, a daughter of John and Mary Oldham of Northumberland county, Virginia. Wineford and William Neville both signed the marriage consent in 1796 of their daughter, Malinda.

(Adapted from "History of Rutherford County, North Carolina")
